# v0.17.0 — A second mother tongue

*27 April 2026*

**TL;DR**

- **[CV2.E1 — Localization](../project/roadmap/cv2-accessibility/cv2-e1-localization/)** epic complete. Six stories shipped (S1, S2, S2b, S3, S4, S5, S5b). The whole product, including the admin workspace, renders in pt-BR when the active user has `locale = 'pt-BR'`. Conversations themselves still respond in whatever language the user types — the localization layer is chrome only.
- **i18n infrastructure (S1).** New `t(key, locale, params?)` helper with a fallback chain (locale → en → crude key + warn). Two resource files (`adapters/web/locales/en.json`, `pt-BR.json`). Hono middleware on both the parent `app` (so `/login` localizes from `Accept-Language` pre-auth) and the `web` sub-app (so authenticated routes use `user.locale`, with ALS scopes nesting cleanly). New `ts(key, params?)` exported helper reads the locale from `AsyncLocalStorage` so JSX components can localize without prop drilling.
- **User picks the language (S3).** New `users.locale TEXT NOT NULL DEFAULT 'en'` column. New Language card on `/me` (universal — not admin-gated like the BRL toggle). `POST /me/locale` validates against `SUPPORTED_LOCALES`; anything else silently writes 'en' as a defensive fallback. Saved/error flow follows /me's existing pattern (key id in URL → resolved via `ts()`).
- **Externalization in seven rounds (S2 + S2b).** ~300 keys flow through ts(): sidebar/layout, login, home, /me, /conversation surface (including conversation header, context rail, composed-prompt drawer, divergent-run sub-bubbles), /conversations browse, /organizations (list + workshop + lifecycle), /journeys (list + workshop + lifecycle), /map (Psyche Map + persona badges + memory section), layer workshops (soul / identity / expression / behavior / persona), /personas, /docs nav, /admin dashboard, /admin/{users, budget, models, oauth}. Module-level constant maps that needed translation moved to render-time helpers (`resolveLayerMeta`, `roleLabel`, `roleHelp`).
- **pt-BR translation (S4).** `pt-BR.json` filled authorially. Voice: "você" + 3rd person, no Lusitanian conjugation. Vocabulary aligned to project register: *Travessias* (not Jornadas), *Espelho*, *Mapa da Psique*, *Elenco* (theatrical metaphor preserved for the persona cast), *Olhar dentro* for "Look inside", *Sobre você* for "About You". Anti-hype lexicon explicitly avoided ("alavancar", "potencializar", "engagement", "thought leader") — vocabulary the project rejects in its own user-facing voice.
- **First non-American tenants (S5 + S5b).** [Antonio Castro](../product-use-narrative/users/antonio-castro/) — 41, mineiro de origem (Lourdes, BH), mora em Floripa (Lagoa) desde 2021, casado com Bia, pai do Tonico (12). Sustenta a casa editorial Pages Inteiras (newsletter quinzenal ~30k, podcast *Carta à mão*, cursos sobre escrita pública sustentável). Saiu do circuito Hotmart depois de um colapso em 2020. Linhagem de três Antônios em três gerações. [Bia Lima](../product-use-narrative/users/bia-lima/) — 38, pediatra plantonista no Hospital São Luís (Floripa), mineira de Sete Lagoas, ex-UTI pediátrica em SP. Carrega o luto recente da Lara (3 anos, sepse), uma proposta pendente de coordenação do PS, um casamento em descuido com Antonio, e um grupo de WhatsApp com Carol e Dani ("As Brutas") que sustenta a vida emocional. 5 personas em pt-BR para Antonio (`criador`, `escritor`, `pai`, `marido`, `filho`); 4 para Bia (`medica`, `mae`, `esposa`, `amiga`).
- **Cross-pollination at the narrative level, isolation at the data level.** The two tenants cohabit the same household — Antonio and Bia are married, share a son, live in the same apartment in Florianópolis — but their mirrors are isolated tenants. The same household-level events appear in both from different angles: the night Bia comes home exhausted appears in Antonio's `marido` conversation (he names what he was avoiding and goes to her) and in Bia's `esposa` conversation (she chooses a three-frame escalation over three weeks instead of a marker DR). The Tonico-on-screens transition appears in Antonio's `pai` (the boy who Googles his father and confronts him with the Instagram) and in Bia's `mae` (the boy who lies twice about Roblox time). Same boy, same week, two tenants, two registers. It's the deepest test the narrative makes of the multi-user model.
- **Loader plumbing for per-tenant locale.** `narrative-loader.ts` reads `profile.md` frontmatter; supported `locale` values are persisted via `UPDATE users SET locale = ?` after `createUser`. Default 'en' for tenants without the field — the original Reilly–Marchetti family unchanged.
- **Antonio promoted to admin** in `ADMIN_SLUGS`. Narratively: he's the technical-leaning member of the Brazilian household, the one who would have provisioned the server and added Bia. Symmetric with Dan in the Reilly house.
- **Test isolation bug fixed.** The narrative-loader tests ran against in-memory SQLite but were writing tokens to the production `.tokens.local` file on disk every run. Each `npm test` overwrote the production tokens with throwaway hashes whose counterparts existed only in the in-memory DB that just got discarded. `LoadOptions.tokensPath?` lets callers override the file; tests now use `os.tmpdir()` paths cleaned up in `afterEach`. Production `.tokens.local` no longer touched by `npm test`.
- **729 tests passing**, up from 689 at v0.16.0. +40 new across i18n unit, locale middleware smoke, /me/locale POST + persistence, pt-BR rendering on /me and /conversation, narrative loader (Antonio + Bia provisioning, identity layers, orgs, journeys, conversations, isolation between tenants, locale plumbing, idempotency on rerun, role reconciliation).

## Where we were

v0.16.0 (*Every layer earns its place*) finished the pipeline-conditional work in CV1.E7. Reception's classification went from 4 axes to 5 canonical + 3 auxiliary. Scope content composed only when reception activated it; `self/soul` and `ego/identity` composed only when the turn touched identity. Out-of-pool suggestion landed for the cases pool-as-constraint silently locked out. The pipeline got thinner, and the system learned to admit the lockouts.

What stayed unchanged: the user-facing surface was English. Every label, every empty state, every confirmation — hardcoded in JSX, untranslatable. The narrative was four American characters in suburban Pennsylvania. The multi-user architecture had been validated with synthetic data, but never against a tenant whose entire chrome and content lived in a second language. Onboarding a non-English-speaking family member was structurally impossible.

v0.17.0 closes that on both fronts. The chrome learns to travel; the narrative grows a household whose register the existing four don't reach.

## What we did

### The rail (S1)

`adapters/web/i18n.ts` exposes `t(key, locale, params?)` with a fallback chain (locale → en → crude key + a `console.warn`; never throws). Two resource files at `adapters/web/locales/{en,pt-BR}.json`. Flat dictionary, dotted keys (`sidebar.brand.title`), simple `{name}` interpolation. Pluralization parked behind `S1b` until a real key surfaces a need — for now, English keys with one/many splits suffice (e.g. `home.continue.earlierThreads`, `admin.dashboard.userOne` / `userMany`).

The hardest piece was reaching the locale from inside JSX components without prop-drilling. Hono JSX is server-rendered synchronously inside route handlers, so the natural path is `c.get('locale')` from the Hono context — but components don't have access to `c`. Threading `t` through every component prop became impractical at the Layout level (every page passes a Layout, every Layout would need the prop).

The fix: `AsyncLocalStorage`. The locale middleware wraps `next()` in a `localeStore.run(locale, ...)` block; downstream code that calls `ts(key, params?)` reads the locale from ALS. Outside any scope (e.g. unit tests of pure components), `ts()` falls back to `DEFAULT_LOCALE`. Idempotent — the middleware registers on both the parent `app` (for /login pre-auth, resolves from `Accept-Language`) and the `web` sub-app (post-auth, resolves from `user.locale`, with ALS scopes nesting cleanly so the authenticated locale wins inside `next()`).

[6 unit tests on `t()`, 3 on `ts()`, 6 smoke tests through `app.request()`.]

### The user picks (S3)

New `users.locale TEXT NOT NULL DEFAULT 'en'` column added to the `users` table via the existing `migrate()` chain in `db.ts`. Default 'en' for the four existing American tenants on upgrade — no behavioral change. `createUser` writes 'en' explicitly on new rows (or whatever the loader detected from frontmatter, post-S5).

The Language card lives on `/me` between the user's name and the currency-display preference. Universal — every user sees it, not admin-gated like the BRL toggle. Uses a native `<select>` with `onchange="this.form.submit()"` for auto-save (same pattern as the radio buttons elsewhere on the page). Two options today: English, Português (Brasil). Adding more is additive — drop a `<locale>.json` file, add the option to the select.

`POST /me/locale` validates against `SUPPORTED_LOCALES`; anything else silently writes 'en' as a defensive fallback so a malformed POST never breaks the row. Redirects to `/me?saved=locale`; the GET handler resolves to the user-locale string `me.saved.locale` ("Idioma atualizado" / "Language updated"). Non-admins allowed (covered by a new test); the BRL toggle stays admin-only as before.

[3 new POST tests, 1 round-trip pt-BR rendering test on /me.]

### The chrome travels (S2 + S2b)

Externalized in seven rounds across two sub-stories:

| Round | Surfaces |
|------|----------|
| S2 (1/6) | ALS scope + layout/sidebar |
| S2 (2/6) | login, home, /me |
| S2 (3/6) | conversation surface (chat shell, conversation header, context rail, composed-prompt drawer) |
| S2 (4/6) | /conversations browse |
| S2 (5/6) | organizations + journeys (list + workshop + lifecycle, plus the shared scope.\* components) |
| S2 (6/6) | personas + docs + map + layer-workshop |
| S2b | admin dashboard + admin/{users, budget, models, oauth} |

~300 keys total. Module-level constants that needed translation moved to render-time helpers — `resolveLayerMeta(layer, layerKey)` in `layer-workshop.tsx`, `roleLabel(role)` in `admin-dashboard.tsx`, `roleHelp(role)` in `admin/models.tsx`. Same pattern: ts() can't be called at module scope (no ALS context), so anything that would have been a lookup table becomes a function called at render time.

A handful of structural decisions emerged in flight and got codified along the way:

- **Bold inline emphasis preserved by chunking.** Where a translatable paragraph wraps a `<strong>...</strong>`, the paragraph splits into part1 / strong / part2 (and sometimes ...partN). Translators control phrasing on each side of the bold marker; the JSX preserves the emphasis itself. Used in workshop help text (organizations, journeys), the persona invitation on the Psyche Map, and the admin/models lede.
- **Saved/error redirect carries a key id, not a localized string.** /me established the pattern (`?saved=name`, `?saved=preference`, `?saved=locale`); /admin/budget adopted it in S2b (`?saved=rate`). The GET handler resolves the id via `c.get("t")(...)`. Avoids leaking English strings through URLs and breaks the assumption that the redirect's writer and the page's renderer share a locale.
- **Inline `confirm()` built via `ts()` with single-quote escaping.** Bubble delete confirms, persona delete confirms, scope-lifecycle delete confirms — all use `ts("...").replace(/'/g, "\\'")` inside the `onclick`/`onsubmit` attribute string. Awkward but unavoidable: `confirm()` is browser JS evaluating a string we render.

[~150 keys in en.json and pt-BR.json each. Existing route tests didn't pin specific copy, so most passed unchanged; the 7 that did pin copy migrated cleanly.]

### pt-BR fill (S4)

`pt-BR.json` filled authorially. Voice rules:

- **"Você" + 3rd person.** Never Lusitanian conjugation (`tu vês`, `vós podeis`).
- **Project vocabulary preserved**: *Travessias* (not "Jornadas"), *Espelho* for "the mirror", *Mapa da Psique*, *Elenco* (theatrical metaphor preserved for the persona cast), *Olhar dentro* for "Look inside", *Sobre você* for "About You", *Mensagem* not "DM" / "msg".
- **Anti-hype lexicon explicitly avoided**: "alavancar", "potencializar", "engagement", "thought leader", "alta performance", "ressignificar", "validar a sua dor". The mirror's editorial posture rejects this vocabulary in its own user-facing voice.
- **Brand names stay English**: "Mirror Mind" (the brand) is preserved; "the mirror" lowercased becomes *o espelho*.
- **Persona / organization / journey keys stay as written**. Identifiers, not strings — they don't translate at the i18n layer. (Antonio's tenant has pt-BR persona keys because that's how a Brazilian author named them; Elena's keys are English because that's how Alisson named hers in 2024.)

A pt-BR rendering smoke test asserts that a user with `locale='pt-BR'` hitting `/me` and `/conversation` sees translated chrome (Sobre você, Preferências, Idioma, Travessias, Mapa da Psique on /me; Digite uma mensagem..., Enviar, Elenco, Contexto on /conversation) and no leftover English chrome on the externalized surface.

### Test isolation fix

A bug discovered mid-flight: `loadNarrative()` writes the bearer-token map to `.tokens.local` at the narrative root every run. Until the fix, the narrative-loader tests ran against in-memory SQLite but persisted tokens to the real file on disk — every `npm test` invocation overwrote the production tokens with throwaway hashes whose counterparts existed only in the in-memory DB that just got discarded. Symptoms surfaced when the user tried to log in as Antonio after a sequence of test runs: the `.tokens.local` had fresh tokens but the live DB had completely different `token_hash` values for the older tenants.

`LoadOptions.tokensPath?` lets callers override the file path. Default stays `TOKENS_FILE` for production. The test harness now generates a unique path under `os.tmpdir()` per `beforeEach`, runs the loader against it, and removes the temp file in `afterEach`. The production `.tokens.local` on disk is now untouched by `npm test`. `readTokensFile`/`writeTokensFile` take the path as parameter; the public `readTokens()` helper reads from `TOKENS_FILE` (unchanged external API).

Recovery for the user's dev DB: the .tokens.local was regenerated atomically (random token → SHA-256 → DB UPDATE → file write) for each affected tenant. The fix landed mid-session; future test runs no longer pollute.

## What this means in practice

A Brazilian user is added to the server. Admin runs `npm run admin -- narrative load` (or, in a real deployment, the existing `user add` admin CLI). They get a token. They open the login page in a Brazilian browser; `Accept-Language: pt-BR` is read by the locale middleware on the parent app, login renders in pt-BR ("Entrar / Cole seu token"). They log in. The auth middleware loads `user.locale = 'pt-BR'` from the DB. The `web` sub-app's locale middleware re-runs and stamps the user-locale on the request context. Every subsequent page renders in pt-BR — the sidebar (Conversa, Travessias, Organizações, Mapa da Psique), `/me` (Sobre você, Preferências, Idioma), `/conversation` (Elenco, Contexto, Modo, Olhar dentro), the admin workspace (Ambiente Admin, Usuários, Orçamento, Modelos, OAuth) for admin users, and so on. The Brazilian tenant operates the entire product in their own language.

The narrative now has six tenants: Dan, Elena, Eli, Nora (English-language Reilly–Marchetti family in Lancaster, PA) plus Antonio and Bia (Portuguese-language Castro–Lima household in Florianópolis, SC). The narrative validates not just "the localization layer works" but "the multi-user model holds when two tenants share a household, an event, a child — and yet operate from isolated mirrors." That had been an unverified architectural claim until v0.17.0. It's now load-tested by content.

## What's next

CV2 (Accessibility) has three more epics queued — WhatsApp adapter (CV2.E2), dedicated public web interface (CV2.E3), voice input (CV2.E4). None of them activated yet. The decision to land Localization first paid: it was the cheapest accessibility win, schema-light, internal-only, and unblocks the largest class of users (anyone whose default language isn't English).

The narrative's "second cut" is open for further extension. The Brazilian household has Antonio (admin) and Bia. The pai, mãe, and filho — Sr. Antônio, Dona Lúcia, Tonico — were considered and parked. They'll come when a feature actually pulls them forward (e.g., elderly tenant testing if and when the mirror needs to adapt to cognitive decline; pre-adolescent tenant when CV3 explores age-appropriate voicing).

CV1 still has plenty open — CV1.E3 (memory), CV1.E4.S2 (attachments), CV1.E7.S6 (semantic retrieval), CV1.E7.S7 (pipeline generalization), CV1.E8 (observability). The choice of what comes next depends on what's pulling — neither a roadmap-driven nor a backlog-driven decision.
