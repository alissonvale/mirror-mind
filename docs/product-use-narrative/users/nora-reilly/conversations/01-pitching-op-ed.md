**User:**
I want to write an op-ed for the April issue about the phone policy the district rolled out in January. Not one of the inoffensive ones. A real one. Help me figure out if the pitch is any good before I take it to Ms. Pratt.

**Assistant:**
Go ahead.

**User:**
Draft pitch: "The district's new phone-in-locker policy was sold as an academic intervention and a student-wellness measure. Four months in, the policy has neither delivered the academic gains the superintendent promised nor addressed any of the actual mental-health concerns it was marketed against. What it has done is create a new set of compliance confrontations between students and teachers that are eating instructional time the policy was supposed to protect. Students deserve an honest accounting of this, not a continued marketing of the policy's supposed successes."

**Assistant:**
Okay. The pitch is close. It has a clear thesis. It has at least two specific claims. It has a stance.

The problems as a pitch, before we even get to the piece:

The thesis is stated as if the claims are already proven. "Neither delivered the academic gains nor addressed the mental-health concerns" — is that something you can actually demonstrate, in writing, with data or interviews, by April? Or is it something you believe to be true that you haven't yet sourced?

**User:**
Partly sourced. I have the district's own quarterly academic-outcomes report that came out in March — they're spinning it as flat or slightly positive, but when you look at the data, the effect is negligible and inside statistical noise. I don't have mental-health data; that'd have to come from interviews with the counselors or from the anonymous student survey the school ran in February.

**Assistant:**
Good. So the academic claim is defensible with a document. The mental-health claim is not, yet. If you file the piece with the mental-health claim un-sourced, you're making an op-ed move that a real paper would flag. Which is fine for *The Trumpet*, but it's a choice.

Two options. One: narrow the claim to what you can source. "The academic gains haven't materialized, and the policy's costs are visible. The mental-health case was thinner than advertised and remains unproven." Narrower, stronger, more honest. Two: keep the broader claim but do the interview work before you file. At least two counselors, a handful of teachers, and the survey data if you can get your hands on it. That's real work. Probably three weeks.

Which do you actually want to do?

**User:**
Option two. The piece is stronger if I can really put the mental-health thing in front of readers.

**Assistant:**
Then the pitch is actually a reported op-ed, not an op-ed. Pitch it that way to Ms. Pratt. "I'd like to do a 1200-word reported opinion piece on the phone policy, filing for the April issue, pulling from the district's Q3 outcomes report, the February student survey, and interviews with counselors and teachers. Here's my thesis, here's my sourcing plan." That is a different pitch and it will land differently.

**User:**
Yeah. That's better. Two more things I want to ask about. First: the opening.

**Assistant:**
Hit me.

**User:**
I keep reaching for "in January, the district rolled out a new phone policy." That's the inverted pyramid opening. Boring. I want to open on a specific scene — a teacher on a Tuesday morning confiscating a phone from a student, the way the hallway got quiet. That's a better first sentence but it's also a loaded scene. Pitch-wise, does it work?

**Assistant:**
The scene is stronger. Scenic openings on a reported op-ed are fine. The thing to check: does the scene carry the piece's actual argument, or is it there because you like it as an image? If the scene ends in a quiet hallway because of an awkward confrontation, and the piece is about the awkward confrontations the policy has created, the scene earns its place. If the scene is just a student-teacher moment that you think is cinematic, it's a vibe-based opening and the editor will push back.

**User:**
It ends with the rest of the class losing five minutes of lecture time because the teacher and the kid had to negotiate whether the phone was actually "out" under the policy. That's the whole piece, in miniature.

**Assistant:**
Then the scene is the right opening. Lead with it.

**User:**
Last one. How honest should the op-ed be about the fact that I have a view? Ms. Pratt always says "opinion pieces still need to represent opposing views fairly." Which I get. But I also don't want the piece to read like a both-sides-ing that's pulling punches.

**Assistant:**
Represent opposing views fairly is different from both-sides-ing. Represent means: steelman the strongest version of the argument for the policy, not the dumbest version, and then explain why you find it insufficient. Both-sides-ing is when you present two positions as if they are equally weighted, regardless of the evidence. You can do the first without doing the second.

In practice: find the policy's strongest defender — not a straw-man defender, a real one — and quote them on the best argument for it. Then show why, even granting their strongest case, the evidence you have points the other way. That's the honest version. That's also the version Ms. Pratt will approve.
