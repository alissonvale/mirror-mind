And every exchange since has been that kind of short. She's not ignoring me. She's also not really here.

**Assistant:**
"Lol yeah" from Amelia, about *Succession*. Okay. You know her well enough to know that's not her normal.

Before we think about what to do, let me ask: what's the range of possible explanations you've been running through?

**User:**
Four. One: she's going through something and hasn't told me. Two: she's pulling back intentionally because she doesn't want the friendship anymore. Three: she's moved into a different friend group and hasn't actively chosen me out, she's just naturally spending time elsewhere. Four: she's noticed that I've been pushing back on some of the newer things she says and she's protecting herself from the friction.

**Assistant:**
Good range. Which do you think is most likely?

**User:**
Probably three, with some of four mixed in.

**Assistant:**
Right. That's my read too, for what it's worth. Let me push on four, because that's the version you have some agency about.

When you say you've been pushing back — what does that look like, specifically?

**User:**
A couple of times in the last two months, she said something I thought was wrong — not terrible, just wrong — and instead of letting it go, I pushed. Not hard, but enough that she noticed. Once it was about a trans athlete thing. Once it was about the Muslim family I told you about. Both times the conversation ended a little cooler than it started. I don't think I said anything cruel. **Assistant:**
Yeah. You're not wrong to push back on things you think are wrong. And — you should know — there's a range of ways to push back, and the way you push is a habit. You go for precision. You also sometimes go for precision that has an edge, when a less-edged version would have done the same work.

What do you actually want from this friendship?

**User:**
I want it to survive her drifting rightward for a year or two. I want to still love her when she's twenty-five, even if she ends up in a different political place than me. I want this not to be a friendship that only works when we agree about everything.

**Assistant:**
That's a serious answer. It's also not trivial to deliver on. A friendship that can survive ideological difference is a real skill and not everyone has it, at sixteen or at forty. You're saying you want to be the kind of person who has it.

Okay. Given that — the edge on your pushbacks is not helping. You can hold positions without needling. Needling is what happens when you're trying to signal to yourself that you haven't agreed, even as the conversation is ostensibly about something else. It's a tell.

**User:**
Yeah. I think I've been doing some of that.

**Assistant:**
Here's a specific thing to try. Next time Amelia says something you disagree with, don't respond to it in the moment. Don't push, don't needle, don't one-line her. Wait. If it's something that genuinely matters, bring it up a day or two later when the conversation isn't in the middle of something else. "Hey — I wanted to come back to that thing you said about —" That gives both of you the room to actually think.

And — more importantly — have the meta-conversation. Not right now, but soon. Say to her: "I feel like we've been short with each other lately and I don't want that to just keep happening. Are we okay?" That's five sentences, maximum. It does the work. She gets to choose whether to name what's going on or deflect. Either way, you've made clear that you noticed and that you care.

**Assistant:**
Both are real. Both might happen. Neither is worse than the slow fade you're currently in. The slow fade is the thing you're actually afraid of; the conversation is just the scarier-looking version of a thing that's already happening.
