**User:**
Staff meeting was awkward today. Jared filed his last feature for the May issue. It's not good. I have to tell him it's not good, or get him to rewrite, or pull it. Every option has complications. Help me think about this.

**Assistant:**
Before we talk about what to say to him — what specifically is wrong with the piece?

**User:**
The thesis is soft. The sourcing is thin. He quotes three people and two of them say basically the same thing. The structure is loose; the fourth paragraph repeats what the second paragraph already said. And the kicker is a version of "what does it all mean?" that doesn't answer its own question. If a staff writer filed this, I'd send it back.

**Assistant:**
Okay. So the piece has real problems. It's not a borderline call. Good to start from there.

Now, separate question: what's complicating the decision?

**User:**
Three things. One, he's the outgoing managing editor and he expected to be EIC. Two, he hasn't warmed up to me since the vote. Three, this is his last issue before he graduates. If I pull the piece, I'm actively diminishing his last contribution to the paper in a way he will read as personal.

**Assistant:**
You are making this harder than it is. Or — more accurately — you are letting the political situation do your editorial thinking for you. Let's separate.

The editorial question: is the piece fit to print as-is?

**User:**
No.

**Assistant:**
Okay. So the piece cannot run as-is. That's decided. The remaining question is not whether to pull it but how to get it to a version that can run.

What are your options?

**User:**
One: ask him for a rewrite with specific feedback. Two: do a heavy edit myself and send him the edited version for approval. Three: pull it and fill the space with something else.

**Assistant:**
Which of those would you do if the writer were someone else? Maya, say.

**User:**
Option one. Specific feedback, rewrite request, turnaround in three days. I'd tell her the thesis needs sharpening, the sourcing is thin, and the kicker needs to actually land.

**Assistant:**
Right. That's the editorial answer. That's also the answer for Jared. The reason it feels harder is not editorial; it's relational. And the relational reason is not a reason to do something different. It's a reason to do the correct thing with care.

So: option one. Here's how.

**User:**
Go.

**Assistant:**
Do not handle it in a staff meeting. Do it by email, sent Monday morning so he has the week to work on it and doesn't think you're ambushing him. Short, specific, professional. Start with one honest thing you think is working — there's always something — then enumerate the three specific things that aren't. The thesis. The sourcing. The kicker. Don't give him a dozen notes; give him the three that will actually move the piece forward. Set a rewrite deadline. End with "I'm available if you want to talk through any of this."

Do not apologize for the feedback. Do not soften the notes past the point of clarity. Do not, under any circumstance, frame this as "the senior editor is being tough on you." Frame it as what it is: the new editor-in-chief doing her job on a piece that needs work before it's ready to run.

**User:**
What if he pushes back.

**Assistant:**
He might. If he does, the correct response is to hold your ground on the editorial judgment and — this is important — stay out of the political register. "This piece needs a stronger thesis before it's ready to run" is a statement you can defend for the next three years. "You're making this hard because you're still upset about the vote" is a thing you would regret saying within a week.

If he escalates further, bring Ms. Pratt into it. Adviser involvement is appropriate when staff-editor conflict is genuinely breaking down. She has handled versions of this before and she will back you up on editorial judgment if the editorial judgment is sound. Which it is.

**User:**
What about the politics between us? Should I say anything about the vote?

**Assistant:**
Not in this email. Not as part of this editorial conversation. Keep the two separate. If you want to address the vote between you two, do that — and I think you should, before he graduates — but not as a way of softening this feedback. Those are two different conversations with two different purposes.

Actually, here's a better move. If you're going to address the vote separately, do that first. Like tomorrow or Friday. Short, honest, acknowledging the awkwardness without apologizing for winning. Then, Monday, the editorial email. Two separate conversations, in order. The editorial feedback will land cleaner if the air between you is slightly clearer.

**User:**

Good.
