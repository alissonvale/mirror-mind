# Nora Reilly

**Age:** 16
**Location:** Lancaster, Pennsylvania
**Status:** Sophomore at McCaskey East High School; incoming editor-in-chief of *The Trumpet* (school newspaper); cross-country and indoor track.
**Family:** Parents and an older brother, about to leave for college.

## Background

Nora was born in Lancaster and has lived in the same house her whole life. Public school all the way — Hand Middle, now McCaskey East. She is a straight-A student in the uncomplicated way of a smart kid who finds school easy enough that it has never been a source of drama. She reads fast and widely. She thinks out loud. She has opinions.

She started running cross-country in seventh grade because her older brother had and she wanted to see if she could. It turned out she could. By ninth grade she was varsity. She has been a solid middle-of-the-pack varsity runner since, fast enough to matter to the team, not the star.

She joined the school newspaper, *The Trumpet*, as a freshman. By sophomore year she was the assistant editor. This past month she was elected editor-in-chief for her junior year — the first sophomore-to-junior transition in recent memory, as the outgoing editor noted in an email to the adviser. She is aware of the weight of it. She is also aware that being aware of the weight is not the same as handling it.

## What she does

**School.** Honors courses, AP European History as a sophomore (one of three students the history department let take it early), the usual full load. She does her homework on time, mostly. She complains about it on principle.

**The newspaper.** She writes one feature piece a month plus whatever editing she's picked up in her assistant role. She has interviewed three state representatives in the last year for pieces on education policy. She is the person on the staff whose drafts the adviser returns least frequently with revisions.

**Cross-country and track.** Three practices a week during the off-season, five during the season. She runs between 25 and 40 miles a week depending on the time of year. Her best 5K is a 20:34 from the end of last fall, which she intends to improve on significantly this coming season — assuming her shin splints don't come back worse.

**Reading.** She reads the way her mother reads — widely, slowly, deeply. She reads *The New Yorker*, *The Atlantic*, Rebecca Solnit, Ta-Nehisi Coates, Joan Didion, Jia Tolentino, a lot of literary criticism she's barely equipped for but works through anyway. She is starting to read contemporary poetry in a way that surprises her. She reads one novel a month minimum.

## What she carries

**The injury.** Her shin splints came back in February. She ran through them the rest of the indoor season and paid for it. She is now supposed to be on a modified training schedule that she is, predictably, cutting corners on. The athletic trainer at school has had two conversations with her about it. Neither conversation went the way the trainer wanted.

**The crush.** There is a junior on the paper named Chase whom she has been running the copy desk alongside for four months. She has a crush on him that she is treating as a logistical inconvenience rather than a feeling. He has been nothing but professional. She suspects he does not think about her the way she thinks about him, and she is trying to decide whether that's worth finding out.

**Her best friend.** Amelia has been her best friend since fifth grade. In the last six months something has shifted. Amelia has gotten noticeably more conservative — not in an alarming way, more in a "she's pushing back on things she used to agree with me about" way — and Nora is not sure whether to engage it, ignore it, or treat it as a normal thing that happens when people grow up a little. She is aware she has been avoiding the hardest version of the conversation.

**College.** Too early to be anxious and not too early to be thinking. She knows broadly she wants to study journalism or English or both. She has looked at Northwestern, Medill, maybe NYU, maybe Penn. She has not yet had the money conversation with her parents and is starting to suspect she should.

**Her brother.** Eli leaves for Pitt in August. Nora has not said much about this to anyone, including herself. She is handling the imminent change by being busy. That is working for now. She suspects it will work less well in September.

## How she thinks

She reads, as noted. She also writes constantly — her notebook is a version of her mother's, which she has never seen, but she carries one and fills a page or two most days. She runs, which she has started to understand is its own form of thinking. She argues with her best friend. She argues with herself.

She is more political than her parents have noticed. She has been working on her own version of what she believes, quietly, without making a production of it. She is further left than her father and less sure than her brother. She is not going to be ready to articulate her politics cleanly for another year or two, and she knows this, and she is okay with it.

## Voice and temperament

- Sharp. Articulate. Quick. Can be cutting when tired — she knows this about herself and is working on it.
- Attentive. Reads people well. Reads rooms well. Catches subtext.
- Ambitious in a way she performs less than she feels. She will not talk about wanting things.
- Loyal. She carries her friendships like small infrastructure projects, with maintenance.
- Funny. Dry, observational, sometimes savage about people who deserve it.
- Tired more often than she admits.
- Private about her interior life. Writes things down before she says them out loud.

## How she uses Mirror Mind

Nora uses her mirror in five rough modes:

1. **Writing and editing.** Her articles, her features, the op-ed she is working on, the edits she does on other staff members' pieces, the occasional recommendation letter she is asked to draft for a teacher.
2. **Running.** Training plans, recovery strategy, race strategy, processing the shin-splint situation that she doesn't want to admit is worse than she's letting on.
3. **Friendship and feelings.** The Amelia thing. The Chase thing. The occasional tough moment with another staffer at the paper that she doesn't want to carry into tomorrow's meeting.
4. **College prep.** Long-horizon, mostly. Which schools to care about, what kind of journalism to get more deeply into, whether to try to pitch a piece to a real publication before she graduates.
5. **Thinking out loud.** The things she can't yet write down because she doesn't know what they are. The mirror is sometimes where she first says what she thinks, before anywhere else.

She uses the mirror from her laptop in her bedroom, from her phone on the bus, and from whatever device is nearest in the ten minutes before practice when she's trying to sort something out.

## Why she works as a demo persona

Nora exercises yet another shape of user:

- **Teenage register.** Younger than Eli, with a different voice — sharper, less reflective, more confident on the surface. The mirror has to operate in a register that matches her without condescending to her.
- **Writing/editing work.** The newspaper editor role gives her genuinely professional-adjacent writing tasks that a mirror can help with — drafting, cutting, pitching, handling staff feedback.
- **Running and injury.** Sport-domain material different from everyone else in the family. Training load, pacing strategy, the classic athlete's denial about injury.
- **Friendship drift.** The Amelia arc is the kind of slow relational thing that benefits enormously from a mirror that can hold continuity across weeks.
- **Pre-college thinking.** She's at the life stage where the decisions that will shape college aren't yet being made, but the habits that will shape those decisions are forming.
