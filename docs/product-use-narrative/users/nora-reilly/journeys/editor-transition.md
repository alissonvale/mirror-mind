# First Semester as Editor

**Status:** active
**Organization:** the-trumpet

## Briefing

The arc of becoming the editor-in-chief of *The Trumpet*. Not just the role but the quieter work of growing into it: setting up the staff, defining what I want the paper to do, handling the dynamics with people on staff who didn't want me to have this job, finding a working register with the adviser, and doing all of that while being a junior who has school, running, college prep, and the rest of her life to keep running.

## Situation

**The summer.** Between graduation and the start of the fall semester, I have to decide on the new staff configuration (EIC, ME, section editors, copy desk, writers), draft a rough editorial calendar for the year, meet with Ms. Pratt at least twice to align on what she expects from me vs. what I intend to do differently, and do whatever prep work is going to spare me from being underwater in September.

**The new staff.** Working theory: Chase as managing editor, Maya as news editor, a rising junior named Kalani on features (she's done some solid feature work and is steady), and someone — still thinking about who — on opinion. Copy desk needs a chief: options are a rising senior named Kenji (capable, not exciting) or promoting someone from the freshman cohort (harder, more interesting, more work). I'm leaning Kenji for stability but still weighing.

**The Jared problem.** He's gone in two months. The question is whether to try to address the awkwardness between us before he graduates or let it fade. My instinct is to address it — not a whole thing, just a short acknowledgment. "I'm sorry it went the way it did in the vote. I'd like to finish the last two issues in a good place." That kind of sentence. I haven't said it.

**The Chase problem (the non-crush version).** Promoting Chase to ME means I'll be working with him closely, and I have feelings about him I haven't named to myself cleanly. The professional question is easy — he's the best candidate, I have to promote him. The personal question is whether I can do the role well while carrying the crush, and whether the crush will subside once I'm working with him in a different configuration or whether it'll get worse.

**What I want the paper to be.** The long answer: sharper, more ambitious, more willing to run long features, more willing to publish real opinion pieces (not the inoffensive kind), more willing to cover the school board and district politics as what they are rather than what the school would prefer they were. The short answer: a paper I would have wanted to read when I was a freshman.

**The test.** The real test of this year is not the first issue. It's the February issue. Anyone can make a good first issue off sheer energy. The February issue — when everyone's tired, the senior staff is starting to coast, and the EIC is the one keeping the standards — is where the year gets decided. I want to set up the structure now so February isn't a crisis.
