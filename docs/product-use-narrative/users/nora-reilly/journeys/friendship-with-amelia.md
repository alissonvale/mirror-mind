# Friendship with Amelia

**Status:** active

## Briefing

Amelia Pasko has been my best friend since fifth grade. We met when her family moved into a house two blocks from mine. Since then: six elementary school years, middle school, freshman and now sophomore year side by side. We have been, for most of that time, the sort of friends whose phones would get a hundred texts a day back and forth, whose mothers know each other, whose families have treated each other's kids like theirs.

Something has shifted in the last six to eight months. Amelia's register has changed. Her political positions have gotten noticeably more conservative — not in an alarming way, more in a "she is saying things she would not have said a year ago" way. Her vocabulary around certain topics has shifted. She has stopped laughing at things she used to laugh at. She has started posting things she would not have posted.

I don't know yet whether this is:
- A normal drift of a friendship as two people grow up in different directions
- A specific influence from a new friend group she has started hanging out with (a senior named Tess and her crowd)
- Something happening in her family that I don't know about
- Some combination of all three

## Situation

We are not fighting. That's both the good part and the hard part.

What I actually notice:

**The decrease in volume.** We text less. Not much less, but noticeably less. The texts are also shorter. A year ago a conversation about anything would run all afternoon. Now it tapers within ten messages.

**The specific moments.** In February, I made a joke about a school-board candidate in the lunch line. Amelia didn't laugh. She said, "I don't think that's that funny." I laughed it off. It was the first time that year she'd told me something I thought was obviously funny wasn't. She had never done that before.

**The positions she's pushing back on.** Not on everything. Specific things. Certain LGBTQ issues she used to be casual about. Trans athletes. A Muslim student we both know whose family had some immigration pressure last fall — Amelia's reaction was noticeably different than mine. Not hostile. Cautious in a way that was new.

**The group she's moving into.** Tess and her friends are not bad people. They are also specifically the friend group that has been most vocal in pushing back on certain editorial choices in *The Trumpet* over the last year. Amelia has started eating lunch with them about once a week. She used to eat lunch with me every day.

**What I haven't done.** I haven't had the conversation. I keep telling myself I'm waiting for the right moment, and I keep knowing, at some level, that "the right moment" is a way of postponing indefinitely. I am also aware that the longer I wait, the more likely the conversation will be a referendum rather than a check-in.

**The honest thing.** I love Amelia. I am also aware that the Amelia I love is, increasingly, a past version of her. I do not know yet whether the current version is a phase, a genuine change, or just growth I need to make room for. I have been treating the uncertainty as a reason to delay. Which is the thing I do, and which I should stop doing.

**What I'm afraid of.** That if I ask her directly what's going on, she will feel interrogated, and the friendship will break on that conversation. I am also afraid that if I don't, the friendship will quietly end without either of us having decided.
