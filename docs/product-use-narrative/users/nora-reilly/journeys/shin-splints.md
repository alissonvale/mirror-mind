# Running Through Shin Splints

**Status:** active

## Briefing

Medial tibial stress syndrome, recurrent. First round in late winter last year, resolved after six weeks of reduced training. Came back in February of this year, partly because I ran through an indoor season I should have sat out of. Came back again for a week in April.

The arc I'm supposed to be on right now: reduced mileage, mostly easy pace, no hard workouts on consecutive days, specific strengthening routine the athletic trainer put me on, running on softer surfaces when possible. I am doing most of this.

## Situation

The live issue is that I am handling my own recovery in a way that is not fully honest, and I know it, and I keep doing it anyway.

What's true:

**I'm running more than I'm logging.** I've been tacking on a mile or two to easy runs and not mentioning it. Coach Rivera would notice if the weekly totals were off; he reads the Strava. I've been pretty careful about the math. Which means I'm managing the deception actively, which is itself a problem.

**I'm ignoring the mild version of pain.** The shin is not quiet all the time. There's a low-grade ache most mornings when I first step out of bed, and a version of it that comes back at the end of easy runs over about five miles. I've been treating both as "background." They aren't background. They are the same signal that escalated into a real injury two months ago.

**I'm not being honest with Ms. Hollins.** The athletic trainer. She has asked me, directly, twice in the last six weeks: "How's the shin feeling?" Both times I said "fine, no pain, mostly quiet." Both times that was not quite accurate. She is being generous. I am using her generosity against my own future.

**The ambition is the problem.** I want to run a 19:XX 5K this fall. I want varsity top five. I want these things enough that I am willing to accept a real risk to my running career to chase them. That calculus is terrible. I know it. I keep doing it anyway.

**The conversation I need to have with myself.** Running through this injury has a real downside: a stress fracture, which would cost me the entire cross-country season, and which the trainer has warned me about explicitly. Running cautiously has a smaller downside: a season where I hit 20:15 instead of 19:XX, and I come back into junior winter healthy. The math is clear. I keep not doing what the math says.

**What I have not yet admitted.** That some of what is driving me to train through this is not actually the running. It is the general sense that I should be producing, and that easing back would be admitting that there are limits I cannot will myself past. Which is an ugly thing to notice about myself, and which I am noticing here because the mirror is where I have the room to say it.
