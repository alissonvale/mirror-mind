**User:**
Laying out half-blinds on the drawer fronts for Mom's desk tonight. Four drawers, all the same width (9 1/4"). I've been going back and forth on spacing. Three tails evenly, or three tails with the end pins noticeably wider. She won't care. I do.

**Assistant:**
What's the drawer front stock — thickness and species? And what thickness are the sides?

**User:**
Fronts are 3/4" cherry. Sides are 1/2" quartersawn white oak.

**Assistant:**
Good. With 3/4" fronts and 1/2" sides you have enough room for either layout to look right. The question isn't structural at that scale — it's visual.

My read: three tails evenly across 9 1/4" gives you a sort of anonymous, balanced rhythm. It reads "competent" but it doesn't say anything. It's the joint you'd see in a decent catalog piece.

Three tails with wider end pins — half-pin on each end at, say, 5/8" to 3/4" baseline-to-shoulder rather than the mechanical ~3/8" — gives the drawer a set of "shoulders" that say the maker was thinking. It's also traditional to period work. That piece already has period bones from everything you've shown me; the wider end pins would echo that.

I'd do the wider end pins. But I'd only do it if you're willing to cut them the same way on all four drawers consistently. Inconsistent pin widths across a set are worse than anonymous even spacing.

**User:**
Ok. What's the tail-to-pin ratio you'd aim for on the interior ones.

**Assistant:**
For this scale and for a hand-cut look that isn't fussy: somewhere around 2:1 tail to pin at the narrow points of the pins. So if your pins taper to about 3/16" at the baseline, your tails can go to about 3/8" or a hair more at their narrowest. Visual rule of thumb, not a formula. Do one drawer front first and lay it against the plan before cutting the other three.

**User:**
Right. Layout tonight, cut one tomorrow evening, check it, then finish the rest on Saturday.

**Assistant:**
That's the right pace. One other thing while we're here: are you cutting tails first or pins first.

**User:**
Tails first. I know the debate. It's what I'm fluent at.

**Assistant:**
Good. Stay with what you're fluent at on a piece this size. The debate matters when you're teaching someone. It doesn't matter when you're building for your mother.
