**User:**
Called Mom on the drive home from the hardware store today. She sounded off. Not dramatic, just — she told me the same thing about a neighbor twice in about four minutes. And she couldn't remember why she had called me on Thursday. I told her not to worry about it. But I don't know. Something sat wrong with me after I hung up.

**Assistant:**
What sat wrong. The fact that it happened, or the fact that you told her not to worry about it?

**User:**
Both, I guess. Mostly the second. I brushed it off because it was easier than the alternative. And I drove the rest of the way home knowing that.

**Assistant:**
Yeah. That's the hard part of this, and you already see it. The small moments where you choose the conversation that doesn't happen over the one that does, because the one that doesn't happen is easier for both of you in the next three minutes, and you don't yet know whether it costs anything in the next three months.

What would the other version of that call have sounded like?

**User:**
I don't know. "Mom, you told me that already." Something like that. And then what?

**Assistant:**
That's the honest question. You stop at "and then what" because there isn't a clean script. The version where you name it to her isn't a version where you then solve anything — it's a version where the thing is named between you, and that changes the relationship, and you don't know how. That's what you're protecting against.

I'm not going to tell you you have to have that conversation. I am going to tell you that the reason you're avoiding it is worth looking at directly, instead of letting it quietly run the next six months.

**User:**
I know. I just don't want to be the one who tells her she's losing it.

**Assistant:**
Nobody does. And you're not going to be telling her anything she doesn't already know somewhere. The people going through this almost always know. What they don't have is someone who will stay in the room with them while they acknowledge it.

One thing you could try — not today, not next weekend, but when the moment is right: instead of correcting the repetition, name what you noticed without making it a verdict. "Mom, I noticed you told me about Joanne twice today. Is that happening more?" That's an invitation, not a diagnosis. It gives her room to say yes or no or "let's not talk about it right now," all of which are useful data.

**User:**
Yeah. I can probably do that.

**Assistant:**
You probably can. And one other thing, while we're here: when the moment does come and you have that conversation, the thing you'll want in the moment is a plan, because you're an ops guy and that's how you love. There isn't one yet. That's ok. The conversation itself is the first move. The plan comes out of what she says, not what you bring into the room.

**User:**
That's useful. I'm going to go sit in the shop for a while.

**Assistant:**
Good call.
