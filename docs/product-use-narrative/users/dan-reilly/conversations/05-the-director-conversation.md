**User:**
Had coffee with Mike Fraser this morning. Current director, the one retiring in two years or so. I asked him what his job actually is day to day. He walked me through a week. I came back with more information than I'd had and less clarity than I expected.

**Assistant:**
What's the part that's less clear now than it was before coffee?

**User:**
I went in with a story in my head. The story was: management is meetings and budget fights, ops is real work, the reason people like Mike end up in his chair is that nobody else would take it. That story is partly true. It's also not the whole picture. And the part that's not — the shaping part, the "deciding what the team spends the next two years on" part — actually sounds kind of interesting. I didn't expect that to land.

**Assistant:**
Yeah. That's worth sitting with. Let's slow it down. When Mike talked about the shaping part, what specifically was he describing. Roadmap conversations? Hiring? What you keep and what you retire? Something else?

**User:**
Mostly the second and third. He said the single biggest thing he does that the IC team doesn't see is choose which fights to have with the CIO, and which to eat. He said that's the job — deciding where you spend political capital, and then being the person everyone blames when it doesn't go the way they wanted.

**Assistant:**
That's a real description. And it's a specific skill — not the same skill as the IC work, not obviously more of it, different. The question for you isn't whether that job is hard. It's whether that particular shape of hard is one you want to be doing.

What was your honest reaction in the moment when he said it?

**User:**
Mixed. Part of me thought "I'd be good at that, I think." Part of me thought "I'd burn out doing that for five years." Both are probably true.

**Assistant:**
Both are probably true, and they're not in conflict. Being good at a thing and being worn down by a thing are separate facts. You can be good at something that also depletes you faster than you can replenish. That's a common trap, especially for people who are used to being competent.

Let me push a little. If you took Mike's job in two years — hypothetically — what would be the two or three things you'd want to change about how he does it?

**User:**
Hm. Let me think. I think I'd want more explicit backing from the team before a fight. Mike absorbs things solo. He takes the hit for us and doesn't really bring us in. I'd want to change that — not to share the blame but to share the actual thinking about which fights matter.

Second, I'd want to protect the deep work. Mike's calendar is meetings back to back. Whatever "deep work" meant in his week sounded like airport time. I'd want to structurally refuse that.

Third — and this is the honest one — I'd want to be doing it from a version of me that has the book or the blog or something going, so it isn't the only thing that's mine. Right now all my identity hooks into the job. If I became director without that, it would collapse into being just the job, and that's when people get bitter.

**Assistant:**
That third one is important. You just named a precondition you didn't know you had. If the "yes" to director depends on having something of your own outside it running by then — writing, the blog, the talks, whatever — then the real question isn't "do I want the director role," it's "am I willing to build the outside-it thing over the next eighteen months so that if the offer comes, I can say yes without it eating me alive."

That reframes the question. Instead of "director or not," it's "what do I need to be true about me by the time the decision actually arrives."

**User:**
Yeah. That reframes it in a way I wasn't seeing.

**Assistant:**
Good. One more. You said the first two things you'd change are about how Mike does the job. The third is about you. That asymmetry is information. The first two are real but they're adjustments — you'd make them as a matter of practice. The third is a load-bearing condition. It's worth naming it that clearly to yourself and to Elena, because the next eighteen months of your decisions should probably route through it.

**User:**
Ok. Alright. I wasn't going to get a clean answer out of this, and I didn't, but I got a better question. That'll do.

**Assistant:**
A better question is usually what progress looks like on this one.
