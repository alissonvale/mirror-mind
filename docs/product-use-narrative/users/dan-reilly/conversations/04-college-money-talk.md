**User:**
I keep putting off the conversation with my daughter about college money. She's been mentioning Northwestern and Medill and I haven't said anything. Sophomore year. I need to have this talk with her in the next month or two. Help me draft what I actually want to say.

**Assistant:**
Before drafting — what's the outcome you want from the conversation? Not the financial plan, the relational outcome.

**User:**
I want her to know the real numbers, and I want her to feel like that isn't a door closing. I want her to trust that I'm being straight with her and not managing her down.

**Assistant:**
Good. Those are the two things to protect. Name them to yourself going in: honesty, and the sense that options remain. If at any point you feel the conversation drifting toward "here's the list of things you can't do," you've lost the frame.

A few structural choices before you draft:

Are you having this conversation with her one-on-one, or with Elena in the room?

**User:**
One on one. Elena and I agreed.

**Assistant:**
Good. Easier for her to react honestly without performing for two parents. Where and when?

**User:**
Probably Saturday morning. The diner on Orange Street. Out of the house, not a walk, not the car.

**Assistant:**
That's a good choice. Public enough to keep it from getting too intense, casual enough to allow actual conversation, and she'll remember it as a proper thing you did together rather than a speech you made at her.

Now the shape. I'd keep this simple:

Open with where you're coming from, not with the numbers. Something like: "I've been listening to you talk about schools and I realized I've been nodding without being honest with you about the money piece. I want to do that before you get any further into imagining specific places."

Give her the number we can put toward this as a family, stated clean. "We have about sixty-two thousand saved. We can contribute maybe fifteen or twenty thousand a year on top of that out of current income without serious pain. That's roughly what a full four years at Penn State would look like without debt. Private schools, unless there's serious merit aid, are a stretch that would mean a meaningful loan on one side or the other."

Then — and this is the part that takes practice — leave space. Don't fill it. Don't rush to "but here are the good options." Let her react. She may be quiet. She may push back. She may ask a question. Whatever comes, you listen before you respond.

Only after her response do you talk about the shape of options: Penn State has excellent journalism; there are small liberal arts schools with strong aid for the right applicant; Medill is not impossible, it just requires either a different financial picture or serious merit scholarship. You're not closing Northwestern; you're being honest about what winning that door would require.

**User:**
What if she's angry.

**Assistant:**
Let her be angry. Don't fix it in the moment. Don't promise anything to make it easier. If she pushes back with "you should have told me sooner," the honest answer is "you're right, I should have." Don't defend. The instinct to defend is how these conversations go sideways.

If she pushes back with "this isn't fair," the honest answer is "you're right, it isn't, and I'm sorry the math is what it is. I'd change it if I could." You're not arguing her out of the feeling. You're staying in the room with it.

**User:**
What's the thing I shouldn't say.

**Assistant:**
A few:

- Don't frame it as a lesson about responsibility or money. She doesn't need a lecture on top of a disappointment.
- Don't compare yourself at her age. "When I was your age I went to community college" is a terrible opener.
- Don't promise you'll figure it out. You won't, and she'll remember you said it.
- Don't use her brother as a comparison point in any direction.
- Don't close the conversation by asking her to "think about it." That puts the resolution on her. The resolution doesn't belong to her.

Close instead with something like: "I wanted you to know the real picture. Nothing has to be decided today. We can talk more whenever you want. I'm glad we did this."

**User:**
Ok. That's a lot but it's the right shape. Let me sit with it. I might draft the opening line tonight and come back to it.

**Assistant:**
Good. One thing to hold: the conversation going well doesn't mean she leaves the diner happy. It means she leaves it trusting you. Those aren't the same thing, and the first one is not the goal.
