# Lancaster Homelabbers

**Status:** active

## Briefing

A small monthly meetup of self-hosting, homelab, and small-scale systems people in the Lancaster / Harrisburg area. Maybe fifteen regulars, another ten who show up a few times a year. We meet the second Wednesday of the month at a brewpub off Route 30. Someone gives a twenty-minute talk on something they built or broke recently, then it's two hours of beer and conversation.

I've been going for about three years. I was invited by a former colleague who knew I'd fit. I've given two talks: one on running Proxmox at home, one on restic backup strategies that actually get tested.

The crowd is mostly men in their thirties to fifties, mostly working in some corner of IT during the day, mostly running more home infrastructure than they probably should. A handful of more serious folks — a couple of SREs at real companies, one retired telecom guy who's forgotten more about networking than I'll ever know, a woman who does embedded Linux for a defense contractor and is often the sharpest person in the room.

It's not a professional network in the LinkedIn sense. Nobody's trying to sell anything. It's genuinely people who like tinkering and want to hear what other tinkerers are up to.

## Situation

The group is in a quiet, healthy place. Attendance is steady. The talks have been good. I'm not actively working on anything group-related other than showing up.

The thing I'm carrying loosely about this organization is whether to do more. A few conversations have come up about whether we should do a small annual event — a weekend thing, maybe thirty people, rented space, a half-dozen talks. I'm interested in principle and cautious in practice. I've seen these things grow past what they were supposed to be and lose what made them worth going to.

I'm also thinking about whether this group could be the first audience for a blog or newsletter if I ever started one. The crowd has the right shape for it: smart enough to not need everything explained, skeptical enough to call out anything lazy. If I were going to start writing in public again, starting with a talk here first would be the honest way in.
