# The College Conversation

**Status:** active

## Briefing

My daughter is a sophomore in high school. College is roughly two years away. The gap between what we've saved, what we can contribute from current income, and what her current ambitions would cost is real. At some point — and I keep putting it off — we need to have an honest conversation about money and school selection.

What we have:
- A 529 account with approximately $62,000 in it, contributed to since she was two
- Current household income sufficient to cover maybe $15-20K/year additional without serious pain
- Modest assets, mostly in retirement accounts I don't want to touch
- A house with equity we could borrow against if we had to

What a realistic four-year cost looks like for the kind of school she seems to want:
- Private school, no aid: $85-100K/year (no)
- Private school, merit aid: $50-70K/year (maybe, depending)
- Public flagship out-of-state: $45-60K/year
- Penn State or another PA state school: $30-40K/year
- Commuting to Millersville or a community college transfer: under $20K/year

The money math says: anywhere private without serious merit aid is a stretch that would mean a meaningful loan burden — either hers, ours, or both.

## Situation

I haven't had the conversation with her. This is the problem.

She's been talking about specific schools — she mentions Northwestern, she mentions Medill, she mentions a couple of small liberal arts places — in the excited, narratively-rich way a teenager talks about a future she's imagining. I've been listening with the smile and not saying anything.

The live questions:

**The honest version.** At some point soon I need to say: here is the number we can put toward this, here is what that means in terms of where you can go without debt, here is what debt would look like, here is what I can and can't promise. I've drafted that conversation in my head more times than I can count. I haven't had it.

**The timing.** If I have it too early, she feels constrained before she's even had the chance to dream. If I have it too late, she's already emotionally invested in schools that aren't realistic. Sophomore year is probably the right window. Spring of this year, most likely.

**The framing.** I want her to feel like this is a conversation about options and honesty, not a door closing. The schools we can afford are not consolation prizes. Penn State has excellent journalism. Millersville has a good English program. There are paths.

**What I'm avoiding.** I'm avoiding it because I don't want to hurt her. I'm also avoiding it because some part of me feels like I should be able to afford anywhere she wants to go, and the fact that I can't is an old, familiar ache I don't love sitting with. That's mine to work on, not hers.
