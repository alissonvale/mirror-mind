# My Mother's Care

**Status:** active

## Briefing

My mother is seventy-six, lives alone in the house I grew up in outside Scranton, and is starting to lose ground cognitively. My father died in 2023. My older brother lives in Phoenix. I'm two and a half hours away in Lancaster. The caregiving responsibility, de facto, is mine.

What I know:
- Mild cognitive impairment, not yet a formal dementia diagnosis. Her GP has been cautious about labeling it.
- She repeats stories within the same visit, sometimes within the same conversation.
- She left the stove on twice in the last month that I know about. Probably more times I don't.
- She still drives. I'm not sure she should.
- She handles her own bills, groceries, medications. She says she does. I haven't verified.
- She's sharp enough socially that her friends at church don't notice anything is wrong. Or if they do, they haven't said.
- She refuses to consider moving. The house is the house.

## Situation

The arc is getting steeper but hasn't broken yet. That's the hardest part to sit with.

The live questions:

**Verification.** I don't actually know how she's doing day to day between my visits. A check every two weeks isn't enough signal. I've resisted setting up cameras or check-in tech because it feels surveillant, but I'm starting to think the alternative is worse.

**Driving.** This is the conversation I'm most avoiding. She can still operate the car, but I've watched her make a few judgment calls lately that wouldn't have happened five years ago. If something happens before I address it, it will be on me.

**Outside help.** There's a local agency that does check-in visits, light meal prep, reminders. She would say no if I suggested it. I might need to start the conversation anyway. Or find a way to introduce it that doesn't feel like being managed.

**My brother.** My brother Tom is useful on phone calls and useless on logistics. That's not an accusation — he lives a continent away and has his own life. But the pattern means I should stop waiting for him to initiate. If I want input, I have to ask for it in specific, decidable questions, not open-ended ones.

**The desk.** I'm building a cherry writing desk in the shop for her, slowly, hand-cut dovetails. It's either a gift for when she moves into assisted living, or a thing she'll never see. I haven't decided which. Both answers are true on different days.

The frame I keep trying to hold: this is a slow arc, not an emergency. I don't have to solve it this week. But I do have to stop pretending the next conversation is going to be easier than the one I'm avoiding right now.
