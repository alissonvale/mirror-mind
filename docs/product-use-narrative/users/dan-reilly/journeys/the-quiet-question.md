# The Quiet Question

**Status:** active

## Briefing

The question of what comes after twenty-six years in operations. This is the slow-burn thread I've been carrying for about two years and have only recently started to actually work on rather than just feel.

The fork, roughly: stay on the IC track another five to ten years at Keystone or somewhere like it; move into management (either at Keystone as the director retires in a few years, or elsewhere); pivot into an adjacent specialty (security, solutions architecture, cloud architecture) that trades some of my depth for a different kind of breadth; start a small consulting practice on the side that could become my main thing; or do something I haven't considered yet.

Age forty-eight. Runway in the current job probably five to seven more years comfortably. Financial obligations: mortgage, Nora's college approaching, ongoing support of my mother, the usual. Not under pressure to change — but also aware that the decisions I make in the next two to three years shape what the back half of my working life looks like.

## Situation

I haven't picked a direction. What I have done, over the last six months, is stop pretending the question isn't there.

The information I've been gathering, quietly:

**Management.** I've had two conversations with the current director about what his job actually is day to day, versus what I see from the IC side. It's worse than I thought in some ways (meetings, vendor relations, budget fights) and better in others (shaping what the team actually builds, longer-horizon thinking). Not yet a clear "yes" or "no" for me.

**Security.** I've been reading more in the space and taking a free-time look at a couple of the certifications. The work is interesting. The culture in a lot of places isn't. TBD.

**Consulting.** A former colleague who went independent three years ago has made it clear he'd throw work my way if I were available. That's tempting and also a trap if I take the bait for the wrong reason (which would be dissatisfaction with Keystone rather than genuine pull toward the work).

**Staying.** The honest version of this option is "staying and writing more" — using the stability of the job to fund a slower second-half of creative output (blog posts, maybe a book, speaking at events like the Homelabbers meetup). Not a career change so much as a shift in what I'm actually investing in.

The thing I keep coming back to: the question isn't "what should I do?" It's "what do I want the next five years to feel like?" I don't have a clean answer to that yet. But I'm at least willing to sit with it now instead of pretending it's not on the table.
