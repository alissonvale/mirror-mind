**User:**
It's six oh four. I just noticed the thing again. Three months in.

**Assistant:**
Yeah. How is it landing today.

**User:**
Worse than usual. I don't know why. Nothing happened.

**Assistant:**
Sometimes that's how it goes. It isn't always responding to something specific. It's been three months and the brain is used to the old rhythm, and some days the absence is louder than others for no particular reason.

What did you do when you noticed?

**User:**
I was working on a problem set and I opened my phone and thought about texting her. I didn't. I scrolled instagram for five minutes instead. Then I came here.

**Assistant:**
Okay. Good that you didn't text her. Also — the scroll is not a great substitute for the thing the texting would have been. The texting would have been stupid but honest. The scroll was neither. You went to your phone because you wanted to feel connected to somebody for a second. Instagram was the next-closest available motion.

**User:**
Yeah. That's accurate.

**Assistant:**
Not a criticism. Just naming it.

Can I ask you something you might not want to answer.

**User:**
Go.

**Assistant:**
When you think about her at six o'clock — is the thought about her, or is it about what the relationship gave you that you don't have right now? The second thing, the shape-of-the-relationship thing, is a much more common version of this than people admit. Sometimes "I miss her" is really "I miss having someone to text at six" and those are different sentences. Not either-or, probably — usually both.

**User:**
That's been kind of the question I've been avoiding. I think it's more the second than I want to admit. Which feels bad.

**Assistant:**
Why does it feel bad?

**User:**
Because it's less romantic. It's also less about her. It makes it sound like she was kind of interchangeable, which she wasn't.

**Assistant:**
The two things can coexist. She wasn't interchangeable, and part of what you miss is the shape of the thing rather than the specific person. That's not a downgrade of what you had. That's what ten months of a relationship creates — a daily structure that becomes something the nervous system expects. When it goes away, you notice the shape of the absence before you can separate what was specific to her from what was specific to having a person.

This is useful, by the way. Not because it makes the six o'clock thing go away — it won't, at least not faster — but because it changes what you're working with. "I miss the texting at six" is something I can help you think about. "I miss Emma specifically, forever" is a harder and less accurate version of the question.

**User:**
What do I do with the texting thing.

**Assistant:**
Real question: who could you text at six? Not as a replacement. Just in the sense that the nervous system wants a person to connect to at that hour, and you've been treating that as a breakup problem when it's also just a regular human need that the relationship was handling.

**User:**
Jake. Mo. My sister, probably.

**Assistant:**
Yeah. None of those are Emma. All three of them are people you like and don't text at six even though you could. See if that changes anything.

**User:**
I can try that. Not Nora though. She's sixteen and doesn't need me turning her into a six o'clock fill-in. That's weird.

**Assistant:**
Fair.

**User:**
Ok. I'm going to go back to the problem set. Thanks for this.

**Assistant:**
Good. It's still going to happen tomorrow at six. That's okay. It is what it is.
