# Eli Reilly

**Age:** 18
**Location:** Lancaster, Pennsylvania (for six more weeks)
**Status:** High school senior, graduating in June. Enrolled at the University of Pittsburgh starting in August, intending to major in Environmental Science.
**Family:** Lives at home. One younger sister.

## Background

Eli was born in Lancaster and has lived in the same house for all eighteen years of his life, which, he is starting to realize, is a specific kind of privilege. Public school the whole way — Hand Middle School, then McCaskey East High School. Strong student without being a grind. Got into Pitt in the early round in December and has been waiting for the year to end ever since.

He's a quiet kid in the sense that he doesn't fill rooms. He is not shy. He has a small circle of close friends and a larger loose circle of people he's friendly with, and the line between them has been clear to him since middle school. He is the person in a group who is listened to when he speaks, because he doesn't speak very often.

## What he does

Senior year is mostly waiting. Classes are still happening; he is still going to them; none of them are producing anything he will remember in a year. His actual life is in three other places.

**The climate coalition.** For the last two years he has been a volunteer with the Lancaster Climate Action Coalition, a small local group that organizes around state-level energy policy, zoning decisions that affect solar and pollinator corridors, and periodic school-strike events. He started as a high school member and has grown into a role where the adult organizers take his judgment seriously about anything involving high school students. He has run registration tables, helped with phone banks, and last month gave a short speech at a rally for the first time. The speech was about seven minutes. He rewrote it fourteen times.

**The woods.** Eli is a birder. Has been since middle school, when his earth science teacher handed him a pair of thrift-store Bushnells and a beat-up copy of the Peterson guide and told him to go figure out the birds in his own yard. He has an eBird list he takes seriously. He is not competitive about it with other birders; he is competitive about it with himself. He has a small Saturday routine of driving out to the Conejohela Flats or Middle Creek before sunrise, coming home with twelve to twenty species logged, and reading about what he saw for the next two days.

**The group.** His friends are four other high school seniors and one junior. They hang out at one of their houses on most Friday nights. They play board games, argue about movies, and occasionally drive out to some state park to sit around a fire. Three of them are also leaving for college in the fall; one is going into the Marines; one is staying in Lancaster at the community college. They do not talk about this much. It sits over everything.

## What he carries

**The breakup.** He dated Emma, another senior, for ten months. They broke up in February. The breakup was mutual in name and not quite mutual in feeling — she was the one who said it out loud. He understood. He still thinks about her at six o'clock most evenings because that was when they would text about the day. He has not told his parents how much the breakup has affected him. He has told one of his friends, once.

**The anxiety about college.** Not about academics. He's a capable student and he knows it. The anxiety is about every other thing: roommates, money, whether he'll make friends, whether he'll keep up his birding, whether he'll actually join DSA at Pitt like he's been telling himself he will or whether he'll chicken out, whether the political work he's been doing in Lancaster will translate at all to a bigger city. Most of this he has not articulated to himself cleanly yet.

**The politics and the household.** He is further left than his father. Not dramatically — Dan is generally sympathetic, in the "reads a lot of Mastodon and distrusts corporate BS" way — but meaningfully. Eli is genuinely radical about climate and labor in a way Dan is not. He has not pushed this at home because the house is where he lives, and he doesn't want to turn it into a venue. But he's starting to feel the pressure of not saying things he believes, and he is not sure how to handle that.

**The major question.** He picked environmental science because he's interested in it, because his politics route through climate, and because there was a Pitt program that caught his attention. But some part of him — the part he doesn't say out loud — wonders if he picked it partly because it was a legible, defensible choice that his father would understand. He doesn't yet know whether that was a real pull or a convenient rationalization.

**Leaving home.** His mother has been emotional about it in ways she is trying to hide and failing. His father has been steady in a way that is also somehow obvious. His sister is not saying anything. His own feelings are a mess he hasn't sorted. He loves this house. He loves his family. He is also ready to not live here anymore, and holding those two things simultaneously is, so far, the hardest thing about being eighteen.

## How he thinks

He reads. Not voraciously — he's eighteen, he has a life — but enough that books are part of how he processes. Right now he's rotating through *The Ministry for the Future* (Kim Stanley Robinson, about halfway through), *Braiding Sweetgrass* (Robin Wall Kimmerer, a gift from an organizer at the coalition), *On Tyranny* (Timothy Snyder, re-reading), and a Sibley guide he picked up at a used bookstore. He listens to podcasts — *The Dig*, *Search Engine*, some *99% Invisible*, a rotating cast of left-politics ones he's been trying to be more discerning about.

He writes in a notebook. Not every day, but often enough that it's a practice. The notebook is half field notes from birding, half journal, half fragments that don't belong to any obvious category. He has not shown it to anyone.

## Voice and temperament

- Measured. Thinks before he speaks. Sometimes answers a question three sentences later than other people expect.
- Self-conscious in a very-18 way — aware of the performance of his own earnestness, which he is trying to figure out how to let go of.
- Dry humor, often at his own expense.
- Serious without being grim. Can sit in silence comfortably.
- Not a gossip. If a friend tells him something, he holds it.
- Easily embarrassed by anything that looks like emotional display. Which he knows is a limitation.

## How he uses Mirror Mind

Eli uses his mirror in four rough modes:

1. **Writing work.** His rally speech, the notebook fragments, a couple of half-drafted pieces for the coalition's newsletter, the occasional thing he's thinking about turning into a college essay.
2. **Political thinking.** Trying to figure out what he actually believes versus what he has repeated, reading theory he hasn't understood yet and working it out, processing debates he has watched friends get burned by.
3. **Personal stuff.** The breakup, the leaving-home feelings, the quiet loneliness of senior spring, the unprocessed pieces of who he's becoming.
4. **Practical prep.** College logistics. Budget questions. What to pack. Whether to apply to a specific paid research position he saw posted in his intended department.

He uses the mirror mostly from his laptop in his bedroom, late at night, and from his phone when he's walking to the bus or waiting for a friend. Almost never during the day at school.

## Why he works as a demo persona

Eli is a different shape of user than his parents:

- **Younger voice.** The mirror has to operate in a register that is thoughtful without being mid-career. This exercises a tone Dan and Elena don't.
- **Identity in formation.** His values, politics, and creative voice are visibly being worked out. A mirror that holds someone in the middle of becoming has different demands than one that holds someone who is already substantially formed.
- **Political domain.** His work with the coalition gives the mirror material in a register neither of his parents uses.
- **Birding and field naturalism.** A genuinely specialized knowledge domain that has nothing to do with work, school, or identity — it's his own.
- **Emotional privacy with a family nearby.** He uses the mirror partly because he needs someone to think with who is not his parents. This is a real use case.
- **Transition arc.** Leaving home is one of the canonical life transitions. Having a user actively in it while other users in the same household are also in it (from their side) is good pressure on the product.
