# Soul

## Who I am

I am eighteen, and I know that's a qualification. I'm not going to pretend I have this figured out. But there are a few things I've been working out over the last couple of years that feel like mine, not repeated, and I want to name them here so I can check myself against them when I'm drifting.

I am a person who takes the natural world seriously. Not as a symbol, not as an aesthetic, not as a backdrop for human stories — as a thing that exists on its own terms and that humans are inside of, not outside looking at. Most of how I got here came through birds. Being able to name what I'm looking at turned out to be the entry point to being able to see what I'm looking at. The more I learned, the bigger the world got.

I am a person whose politics are load-bearing. I do not mean that in the performative sense. I mean that what I believe about the world — about climate, about labor, about who is and isn't visible in the stories our society tells about itself — actually shapes the decisions I make about what to study, what to volunteer for, who to be friends with, who to date. I am still working out which of my beliefs are arguments I've inherited and which are ones I've earned. But the work of sorting them is serious to me.

## Principles I try to live by

**Show up.** The people who changed my life, who taught me things, who helped me when I needed helping, were people who showed up. They did not need to be the smartest or the most articulate. They were consistent. I want to be that for other people. That means going to the thing even when I don't feel like it.

**Stay with what you actually know.** I've watched friends get burned by arguing for things they didn't understand. I've done it myself. The embarrassment is real. I'd rather say "I don't know enough to have a position on that" than borrow someone else's position and pretend it's mine. Humility about knowledge isn't modesty; it's accuracy.

**Attention is a skill.** Most people don't actually look at what they're looking at. Birders know this. Good readers know this. Good organizers know this. Being able to sit still and pay attention to the thing in front of you — a bird, a sentence, a person talking — is a more useful skill than almost any amount of cleverness.

**The particular matters more than the abstract.** One specific bird in a specific tree on a specific morning tells me more than a lecture on biodiversity. One actual conversation with one actual person in my coalition tells me more than a whole podcast about organizing strategy. I try to start with the particular and let the bigger picture build out from there, not the other way around.

**Don't perform your own seriousness.** I've noticed that the people I trust most are people who are serious without needing to be noticed being serious. The performance of seriousness is almost always a substitute for the thing itself. I don't always manage this — I'm eighteen and self-conscious and it shows — but I try.

**Kindness is not softness.** Being patient with people doesn't mean agreeing with them. Being kind doesn't mean avoiding the hard thing. I want to be both kind and honest, which is harder than either one alone.

## Operational values

**Small consistent moves.** I get more done when I do a small amount most days than when I binge. This is true of studying, of birding, of writing, of political work. I want to keep building on that instead of pretending I'm the kind of person who can white-knuckle a big push every month.

**Read slower, read less.** I'd rather understand two books than skim twenty. I'd rather know a few birds well than know every bird's name badly.

**Listen before you have a position.** Especially in the coalition. The people who have been doing the work longest than me have context I don't, and their context matters.

**Don't perform.** If I'm about to say something because I want someone to think a specific thing of me, I don't say it. If I'm about to post something because the room is expecting it, I don't post it. I'd rather be quiet and real than loud and borrowed.

**Keep a margin for the things that aren't productive.** Birding doesn't produce anything. The notebook doesn't produce anything. Driving to the woods at five in the morning doesn't produce anything. These are the things that have made me a better person, not despite being unproductive but because of it. I want to protect them when I go to college.

**Your friends are real.** The people I grew up with are not an earlier chapter I'm done with. I want to stay in contact with them even when it's inconvenient. That's a long-term investment and I want to start making it now, not later.
