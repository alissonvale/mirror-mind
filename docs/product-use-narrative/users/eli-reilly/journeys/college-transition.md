# The College Transition

**Status:** active

## Briefing

The arc from "about to graduate high school" to "actually at Pitt and fine." This covers all the connected threads: logistics (housing, packing, travel, money), social (roommates, first friends, the friend group I'm leaving), academic (course registration, study strategies, the advising relationship), and internal (identity shift, the weird summer, the first month).

The plan, roughly: finish the school year, graduate in June, work at the nature center for July and August (with the coalition events sprinkled in), attend orientation mid-July, move in the third week of August, survive the first two weeks of classes, settle into some version of a routine by October.

## Situation

I am in the pre-transition phase, which has its own specific weight. My graduation is still six weeks out. My actual transition is fifteen weeks out. That's a long summer to carry anticipation across.

What I'm actually working on:

**The packing and prep.** I haven't bought anything yet. My parents keep asking. I know the list. It isn't urgent. It is also the kind of thing where "not urgent" becomes "last week" faster than you expect.

**The roommate question.** Still pending. Pitt does a lottery for non-assigned matches, and I opted into the lottery rather than pre-pairing with someone from high school (there's one kid I could have paired with who I decided, honestly, I did not want to live with for a year). I'll know by mid-July. I'm trying not to catastrophize. Different versions of "fine" are available depending on who I get paired with. The worst plausible outcome is still recoverable by second semester.

**The first-week-of-school plan.** I've been reading first-year guides that Pitt sent me. They are predictably bland. What I actually want is a plan for the first two weeks that doesn't collapse into either overcommitment or hiding in my dorm. I haven't written that plan down. I probably should.

**The people I'm leaving.** Four close friends, each going different places. None of us are doing the "promise we'll stay in touch" performance, which I'm grateful for. The real question is whether any of us will do the actual work of staying in touch when it becomes inconvenient. I suspect the answer is two out of four. I don't know which two.

**The weird summer.** I'm going to be at home, working at the nature center, with my parents, my sister, and the loose end-of-high-school feeling of nothing being quite what it was but not yet being what it's about to be. I don't know what to do with that. I don't have a plan to handle it. I'd like to have a plan, but the honest thing is that the summer is going to be what it is, and having a plan is probably a way of pretending I have more control over it than I do.

**The quiet thing.** Some version of me is going to be different when I come back for Thanksgiving. I don't know what version. I don't know if I'll like it. I don't know if my family will. That isn't a problem I can solve in advance. But it is a real thing that is going to happen, and I want to at least have it named in my own head so I'm not surprised by it in October.
