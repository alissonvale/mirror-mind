# Lancaster Literacy Council

**Status:** active

## Briefing

The Lancaster Literacy Council is a small nonprofit that has been providing adult literacy services in Lancaster County for about forty years. It serves roughly eighty active adult learners at any given time across three program tracks: basic adult literacy (native-English-speaking adults reading below an eighth-grade level), English as a Second Language (working-age immigrants, mostly from Central and South America, some from Eastern Europe), and workforce/citizenship preparation (overlapping populations, focused on specific credential or status goals).

Staff: three paid (executive director, program manager, volunteer coordinator) plus a small roster of part-time contract tutors for the workforce track. Roughly forty trained volunteer tutors handle most of the one-on-one and small-group work. A twelve-member board provides governance and a substantial share of operational fundraising.

Budget in the low-seven-figures, funding patchworked across state adult-ed grants, two foundation partners, a yearly gala, workplace giving programs, and direct individual donors.

I have been a volunteer since 2018. I started as a Saturday morning tutor (my first learner was a Salvadoran man studying for the citizenship exam; we passed it together two years in). In 2021 the executive director asked me to join the board. I chaired the program committee starting in 2023 and am now on the strategic planning subcommittee. I'm in the second year of my second three-year term.

I still tutor one learner — currently a woman from Honduras working toward citizenship and better employment as a pediatric-office medical assistant.

## Situation

The council is at a real inflection point, and most of the board knows it without fully saying so. Three things are happening at once.

The state adult-education funding formula was quietly restructured last year in ways that will hurt small providers over the next two cycles. The council's largest traditional grant is expected to shrink by somewhere between fifteen and thirty percent over the next three years. Nobody has yet modeled what that means for staffing and programming.

At the same time, a large national foundation has put out a three-year grant opportunity focused on workforce literacy for immigrant and first-generation populations. The opportunity fits the council's work well but would require a significant reframing of how it describes itself to funders and a commitment to metrics the council has historically been uncomfortable with. Applications are due in October. The executive director is enthusiastic. A handful of board members (myself included) are cautious.

Finally, the executive director, who has led the organization for eleven years, has begun to hint that she may retire at the end of the fiscal year after next. No formal announcement. But she has said it out loud to three of us, and the board will need to be quietly preparing for a search within the year.

I am centrally involved in two of these — the grant decision and the executive-director transition. The strategic planning work that was supposed to be a tidy year-long process has turned into something that has to hold all three of these threads at once.
