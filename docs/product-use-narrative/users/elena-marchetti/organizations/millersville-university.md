# Millersville University

**Status:** active

## Briefing

Millersville University is a public state university about twenty minutes southwest of Lancaster, part of the Pennsylvania State System of Higher Education. Undergraduate enrollment in the seven thousands, modest graduate programs, strong teaching mission. Not a research powerhouse; a solid teaching institution with some serious scholars scattered through its departments.

I have been in the Department of English for eleven years. I came in tenure-track in 2013, earned tenure and promotion to associate in 2020. I teach a mix of undergraduate and graduate courses, a rotation that typically includes one 300-level American literature survey per year, a graduate seminar each fall (part of the department's small MA program), and the occasional composition course when the department needs it.

My committee assignments rotate. The steady two are graduate curriculum (where I've served for four years) and faculty governance (where I'm in the middle of a three-year term). I advise three to five MA students at any given time and serve on undergraduate honors thesis committees as needed.

My scholarship: one monograph (*The Domestic Hour*, Nebraska, 2016), around a dozen peer-reviewed articles and book chapters since, and a second book in progress that has become the slow thread of my recent years.

## Situation

The department is in a quiet crisis that nobody has named a crisis. Enrollment in traditional literature courses has been softening for years. Applied writing courses, digital humanities, and a new certificate in "public-facing writing" are growing. A faction of faculty wants to restructure the major around the growing offerings; another wants to protect the traditional core. The division is collegial on the surface and tense underneath.

I sit on the governance committee that will have to make a recommendation in the next academic year. I am closer personally to the second faction — I believe the kind of slow reading that American literature asks for is not interchangeable with the skills that "public-facing writing" builds, and I think the department loses something important when it pretends otherwise. I am also philosophically committed to pluralism and suspicious of any version of this argument that treats the other side as a threat. The result is that neither faction is confident I'll be with them when it counts.

Separate from the curriculum fight: tenure and promotion to full professor require another book. *The Domestic Hour* was that book for my last promotion; *Regional Imagination* needs to be the next one. The department is not pressuring me on the timeline, which is generous — but I am aware that a book five years in progress looks different from a book two years in progress, and that difference will eventually matter.

The work I actually love has not changed. Teaching a seminar that lands is still the best week in a good month. Reading a graduate student's chapter and helping them see what it's doing is still the work I signed up for. The weight is mostly in everything around that work, not the work itself.
