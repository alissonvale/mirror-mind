**User:**
It's the second week of May. Eli graduates in about six weeks. He has a summer job lined up at a nature center. He leaves for Pitt the third week of August. I've been handling it okay on the surface and not okay underneath and I need to actually think about this instead of letting it keep being background.

**Assistant:**
Okay. Let's slow this down. When you say handling it on the surface and not okay underneath — what's the actual texture of the underneath?

**User:**
The thing I keep noticing is that I watch him do ordinary things now as if I'm archiving them. Breakfast, putting shoes on, taking out the trash. I know I'm doing it. I can't stop. It doesn't feel bad exactly, but it feels like something I'm not supposed to be doing this much. Like I'm grieving something that hasn't happened yet.

**Assistant:**
You're grieving something that hasn't happened yet because it has already started happening. Anticipatory grief is its own register; it is not the same as ordinary sadness about a coming transition. You are responding to something real. The archiving is your attention doing what attention does when it knows the object is about to change form.

It is also, I think, something you're doing alone, which is making it heavier than it would otherwise be.

**User:**
Yes. Dan is feeling it too but he's not going to say anything. He's going to be steady and make Eli's last summer nice and not process any of it out loud. That's how he does everything that matters to him. I'm going to have to initiate if I want the conversation, and I haven't initiated.

**Assistant:**
What's stopping you?

**User:**
Honestly? Part of it is that I know Dan will try to fix it, or try to be useful, and neither of those is what I want. I want him to sit next to me and say "yes, I know." I don't trust either of us to steer the conversation to that instead of to logistics.

**Assistant:**
That's a specific thing you can ask for, and the asking is the skill. If you begin the conversation without telling him what you need, he will default to what he is good at, which is being useful. That is not a failure on his part; it is a failure of the conversation's setup.

What if you said something like — and these are my words, obviously, you'll find yours — "I want to tell you about how I'm feeling about Eli leaving, and I want you to not try to fix it. I don't want solutions or a plan. I just want you to be with me in it for a while." That's a clean opening. It tells him what he can give you that he wouldn't otherwise think to give. It protects him from his own instinct to be helpful in the wrong register.

**User:**
I could do that. It would feel slightly artificial. I would still do it.

**Assistant:**
Slightly artificial is fine. Marriages develop shorthand over twenty-one years, and shorthand fails in situations that are outside the ordinary grammar. This is one of those situations. The artificial phrasing is the tool. Later, when you've had the conversation, you won't need it.

**User:**
Okay. There's also something else. I've been writing about this — in the essays — and the essays are starting to feel like the real place where I'm actually processing it. More than with Dan, more than with anyone. That feels true and also vaguely disloyal.

**Assistant:**
Which is true?

**User:**
The true part. Writing is how I think. It always has been. The disloyal part is a story I'm telling myself. I think it comes from a sense that the "right" way to work through Eli leaving is in the room with Dan, and that working through it on the page is somehow a substitute. But that's not how I work. I have always processed the largest things first in writing and then in conversation with Dan, and the writing doesn't replace the conversation. It makes the conversation possible.

**Assistant:**
Yes. That's accurate. And you just talked yourself out of the disloyalty framing without me having to argue against it. Which means the framing wasn't actually doing work for you. Let it go.

**User:**
One more thing and then I think I'm done for this morning. Nora.

**Assistant:**

**User:**
She hasn't said much about Eli leaving. I don't know if she's protecting herself or protecting me or just handling it her own way. I keep not bringing it up because she seems to want to keep it in a certain kind of suspended state. My instinct is to follow her lead. My other instinct is that I'm going to regret having followed her lead if the first time we talk about it substantively is in November.

**Assistant:**
Both instincts are worth something. The second one is the one that's going to cost you if you ignore it.

Here's a version. You don't have to have The Big Conversation with Nora. You can have a quieter one, ideally in a moment that isn't a scheduled sit-down — a drive, a walk, cooking dinner. Something like: "I realized this week that we haven't really talked about Eli leaving. I'm not asking you to talk about it right now if you don't want to. I just wanted you to know I'm paying attention to it, and I'm here whenever you're ready."

That's not a demand for a conversation. It's a door.
