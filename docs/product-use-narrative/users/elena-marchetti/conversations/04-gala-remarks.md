**User:**
I need to draft my remarks for the fall gala. Eight to ten minutes, from me as a board member, following the executive director's bigger remarks. Audience is roughly two hundred — donors, community leaders, a few funders, most of the staff, a handful of learners willing to be in the room. I want to say something more substantial than "thank you for coming." I don't want to say something that's in tension with what the council actually is. Help me think about what to say.

**Assistant:**
Start with what you most want the people in that room to understand when they leave. Not what you want to say. What you want them to understand.

**User:**
I want them to understand that the work we do is slow. That literacy isn't a conversion event, it's a six-year relationship between a tutee and an organization. That the outcomes that show up on a spreadsheet are always the trailing indicator of something that happened between two people over many Saturday mornings. I want donors who showed up expecting an inspiring story with a clear arc to leave with a slightly altered sense of what they're actually funding.

**Assistant:**
Good. That's the thesis. Now a harder question: how much of that can you say without sounding like you are critiquing the genre of the gala speech?

**User:**
That's the trap. If I say "literacy isn't a conversion event" in those words I sound like I'm lecturing the room. I have to do the thing, not describe the thing.

**Assistant:**
Yes. And what "doing the thing" usually looks like is that you open with a specific particular — one Saturday, one tutor, one learner, one actual moment — that the rest of the remarks are a slow widening of. The donors leave understanding what you wanted them to understand, but they experience it as having been with you on one particular morning, not as having been instructed.

Who's the particular you would draw from? Without naming the learner, obviously.

**User:**
There's a tutor-learner pairing I can draw from — a retired high school teacher, Barbara, who's been tutoring for eleven years now. She has been working with one woman in particular — I'll describe her obliquely — who first came to the council in 2019 and is now, after a slow arc through basic literacy, ESL, and workforce prep, finishing her first year as a medical assistant. Barbara has been with her the entire time. That's the relationship. It's the one I'd draw from. The donors don't need to know which learner; they need to know Barbara's steadiness.

**Assistant:**
Perfect. That's your opening. Don't lead with "we do slow work." Lead with Barbara on a Saturday morning in 2020, during lockdown, doing tutoring through a phone screen with a learner who was afraid of the device. Lead with the specific shape of her Saturdays over eleven years. Let the implicit argument build.

Then — and this is where the remarks do their real work — you pivot out of the story, not into an abstraction, but into a slightly widened frame. Not "this is what the council does" but something closer to: "Everything the rest of this gala is going to celebrate — the numbers we'll put on the screen, the outcomes we'll describe, the jobs we'll point to — is the trailing wake of what Barbara has been doing on Saturday mornings for eleven years. I want us to keep it in that order."

**User:**
That's close to what I want. Let me try drafting a specific version.

"I want to tell you about Barbara. Barbara is seventy-one. She was a high school English teacher for thirty-one years, and she has been a volunteer tutor at this council for the last eleven. Most of you have never met her. She does not speak at galas. She will not speak tonight. But almost every Saturday morning since 2013, Barbara has sat across from an adult who is learning to read, or learning English, or preparing for a citizenship exam, or working toward a credential, and she has given them the particular, patient, uncelebrated hour that is the whole substance of what the Lancaster Literacy Council actually does."

**Assistant:**
That's the opening. Keep writing.

**User:**
"I want to say that because the rest of this evening is going to be very different in shape. You are going to hear numbers. You are going to hear outcomes. You are going to hear about the eighty-three learners we served last year and the twenty-one who earned credentials and the forty-three who passed a citizenship exam. Those numbers are real, and they are worth celebrating. But they are also the trailing wake of something that happened, one hour at a time, between a Barbara and a learner, on a Saturday morning, in a quiet room. That is the work. Everything else is the way we describe it to each other."

**Assistant:**
Yes. And now you have a choice. You can close here, which would be strong, and would not overreach. Or you can widen it one more notch and speak directly to what your ask of the room is. I would do the second, but quietly. Something like:

"When you write a check tonight, that's what you're writing it toward. Not an outcome. A Saturday morning. And another one. And another one after that."

That's the kind of close that doesn't perform a close.

**User:**
Yes. That's it. I'll tune the middle but the shape is right.

**Assistant:**
One note: don't edit it further once it reads right. Gala remarks that have been polished fourteen times start to sound polished, and the room hears it. This is a speech that wants to sound like you, not like a speech.

Understood. Thank you.
