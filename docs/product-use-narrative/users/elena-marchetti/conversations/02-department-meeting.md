**User:**
Just got out of the department meeting. It didn't go well. Ruth and Jim were both there, Ruth made her case for the restructure again, Jim responded harder than I've seen him respond before, and the conversation got tense in a way the chair couldn't really redirect. I'm back in my office. I have an hour before my seminar. Help me figure out what I actually saw in that room.

**Assistant:**
Start with what Jim did that surprised you. You said harder than you've seen him before.

**User:**
Yes. Jim doesn't escalate. He's usually the calmest person in the room. He said — and I'm paraphrasing, but close — "you are treating our enrollment numbers as the single data point that matters, and you are not being honest about why those numbers look the way they do." That's harder than his usual register.

**Assistant:**
That's a specific escalation. He didn't attack the restructure's substance. He attacked its honesty. That's different from a disagreement about direction. It's an accusation about the terms of the conversation.

Is the accusation fair?

**User:**
Partly. The enrollment data does get used rhetorically by the restructure faction in a way that flattens the actual story. The gen-ed changes over the last eight years redirected a huge share of incoming students away from literature courses. The first-year advising shifts did the same thing. The new digital humanities courses have been promoted in ways our literature courses haven't been. If you held all of those things equal and looked at the remaining variation in enrollment, the case for a traditional-literature program would look different.

But Jim didn't quite say that. He said something close to it, but he framed it as an accusation, and that changed what happened in the room.

**Assistant:**
So Jim is substantively right and tactically escalated. That's worth separating cleanly. You can agree with him on the substance without endorsing the way he said it. That matters for what you do next.

What happened after he said it?

**User:**
Ruth went defensive. She said the data is what it is, and that pretending there's some cleaner version of the data we could retrieve is wishful thinking. She's also partly right. The underlying factors are not cleanly separable, and at some point you have to make decisions based on the data you have.

The chair tried to bring it back to process — "let's have the governance committee take this offline" — and everyone sort of nodded and the conversation ended without ending.

**Assistant:**
Okay. So the real damage is that two of your colleagues escalated against each other in public, neither of them was wrong, and the unresolvable shape of their disagreement is now visible to the whole department. The governance committee inherited a hotter room than it had last week.

What's your read on where you are now, personally?

**User:**
Both of them are going to want to talk to me in the next few days. Probably separately. They both think I can be brought onto their side. Neither of them is going to get what they want from me, and that's going to cost something with both of them.

**Assistant:**
Yes. And you're going to have those conversations anyway, because avoiding them would cost more.

Can I suggest something for the shape of those conversations? When Ruth talks to you, I'd be honest with her that you think Jim's substantive point is partly right. You don't have to agree that the restructure is wrong. You can say: "the data doesn't tell a single clean story, and I don't think the committee can treat it as if it does." That's not an endorsement of the preserve position. It's a statement about what the committee owes the department.

When Jim talks to you, I'd be honest with him that his escalation didn't help. Not as a reprimand — he's already working on processing that, I'd bet — but as a colleague who wants him effective in the coming year. If he's going to be the preserve faction's most credible voice, he cannot burn his restraint credibility in meetings that are not the decisive ones. You would be doing him a kindness by saying that.

**User:**
I can do those conversations. I don't want to, but I can.

**Assistant:**
One more thing. You said neither of them is going to get what they want from you. True. But both of them are also giving you the department's actual situation in the version they can articulate. Jim is telling you that the conversation has been dishonest in a way that's been costing him. Ruth is telling you that the department can't keep refusing to adapt. Both are reporting real information. Your job this year is partly to build a committee recommendation that honors what each of them is actually telling you, rather than the position they think they're defending.

**User:**
That's the useful frame. Let me write down a few notes before my seminar and come back to it tonight.

**Assistant:**
Good. And — for what it's worth — you handled today by not saying anything you'll need to walk back. That's not nothing.
