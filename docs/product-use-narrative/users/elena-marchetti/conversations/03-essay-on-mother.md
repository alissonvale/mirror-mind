**User:**
I have a draft of the essay about my mother. The closer-to-done one. I've been sitting with it for three weeks. I want to read a passage to you and think out loud about whether it's honest or whether it's using the shape of honesty to avoid something harder.

**Assistant:**
Read me the passage.

**User:**
"In the week before she died she stopped talking about the things she had been talking about for eight months. The cancer, the treatment, the appointment schedule, the careful budget of what she had energy for — all of that, which had become the vocabulary of our calls, went away. Instead she wanted to talk about the curtains in my sister Regina's dining room, and about a girl she had known in sixth grade whose name she had not mentioned in forty years, and about whether my father was eating enough when she was in the hospital. I let her. I did not ask her to return to the subjects I was ready for. I have thought about that choice many times since, and I do not know whether it was an act of love or an act of cowardice."

**Assistant:**
That is a finished passage. The writing is doing what the passage needs it to do.

What's the feeling you're trying to locate when you say you can't tell if it's honest or if it's using the shape of honesty?

**User:**
The last sentence. "An act of love or an act of cowardice." I wrote that sentence three weeks ago and it sits well on the page but I am increasingly suspicious of it. It has the rhetorical balance of a finished thought. I don't think I've finished the thought. I think I wrote a sentence that sounds like I've finished the thought because finishing it would be harder.

**Assistant:**
Good. You've named the actual problem. The sentence is well-made, and you are suspicious of well-made sentences at the hinges of your own honesty, because you know how easy it is to substitute a good sentence for a real one.

Let's slow it down. If you had to say — not write, just say, in this conversation — what you actually think now about that choice, what would you say?

**User:**
I think it wasn't love or cowardice. Those are both framings that make it about me. I think I let her talk about the curtains because it was what she wanted, and because it was not about dying. She knew she was dying. She did not need me to make her talk about it. I think I made it about me later, because later I needed a story about what I had done. The honest version is that in the moment it wasn't a choice I made. It was just what happened.

**Assistant:**
That is a different sentence. Notice what it does that the original doesn't: it refuses the binary. The original sentence's structure — love or cowardice — is making the passage a moral self-assessment. The real sentence is making it an act of attention to what your mother wanted. Those are not the same essay.

**User:**
No. They aren't. And the first version is the easier essay. It performs a certain kind of authorial reckoning. The second version doesn't perform anything. It just says what was true.

**Assistant:**
Yes. And — you know this — the second version is harder to write because it is less writerly. It doesn't close with a balanced phrase. It closes with something closer to a shrug, which doesn't look like literary work even though it is, in this case, the actual work.

You have a choice about which essay this is. I'm not going to tell you which one to write. I will tell you that the first version is publishable and won't embarrass you and will be read by anyone who reads it as honest. The second version is harder to land on the page, more vulnerable, and truer to what you actually think happened. Both are defensible. They are not, however, the same essay.

**User:**
I know which one I want to write. I just need to admit that I want to write it.

**Assistant:**
Then that's the work for tomorrow morning.

**User:**
One more thing. When I write the second version, I'm going to want to add commentary that protects the reader from how plain it is. Something like, "and maybe this is what grief does, it substitutes its own structure for the unbearable plainness of what actually happened." Something that reaches for a literary frame. Don't let me do that.

**Assistant:**
Noted. If you bring me a draft with a sentence like that in it, I'll tell you it's there.

**User:**
Okay. Thank you.

**Assistant:**
Go walk for a bit before you write. This is a piece that doesn't want to be revised in the same sitting as this conversation.
