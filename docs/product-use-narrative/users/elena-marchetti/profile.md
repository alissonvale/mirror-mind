# Elena Marchetti

**Age:** 47
**Location:** Lancaster, Pennsylvania
**Occupation:** Associate Professor of English, Millersville University (11th year, tenured)
**Volunteer:** Board member and tutor, Lancaster Literacy Council
**Family:** Married 21 years. Two teenagers at home.

## Background

Elena grew up in Havertown, just west of Philadelphia — the middle of three daughters in an Italian-American family whose grandparents had come over from Calabria in the 1920s. Her father was a high school chemistry teacher, her mother ran the front of house at a small Italian restaurant her uncle owned. Books were not a given in the household, but reading was protected: her mother had never finished high school and held her daughters' education as something close to sacred.

She went to Villanova on a partial scholarship, majored in English, stayed for an MA, then went to Penn for the PhD on American literature and finished in 2011 with a dissertation on women's domestic fiction between the wars. She married Dan in the middle of the PhD — they'd met at a wedding in 2003 and gotten engaged the following year. She held her maiden name professionally; she's Dr. Marchetti at work and Mrs. Reilly on a handful of school forms she fills out once a year.

She took the job at Millersville in 2013, when a tenure-track line opened at a state school near enough to raise a family without the Penn-level demands. It was the right trade at the time. Eleven years later, it still mostly is.

## What she does

Elena teaches American literature, primarily from Reconstruction through the mid-twentieth century, with a specialization in women's domestic fiction and literary regionalism. A typical semester: one 300-level survey, one graduate seminar (she's one of four tenured faculty qualified to teach in the MA program), and either a composition course or a topics course depending on department need.

She publishes steadily but not prolifically. One monograph based on her dissertation, *The Domestic Hour* (University of Nebraska Press, 2016). About a dozen articles and book chapters since. A second book in progress — *Regional Imagination: Place, Labor, and the American Middle Brow* — that she has been working on for a little over five years now and that she is beginning to suspect is going to take another two.

Outside the classroom and the writing, she serves on two university committees (graduate curriculum, faculty governance), advises a handful of MA students, and reviews manuscripts for two journals.

## Where else her time goes

For seven years she has volunteered with the Lancaster Literacy Council, a small nonprofit that provides adult literacy tutoring — basic reading, ESL, workforce literacy, citizenship prep. She started as a Saturday morning volunteer tutor, paired mostly with immigrant adults working toward citizenship or better jobs. Three years in, the executive director asked her to join the board. Two years after that, she chaired the program committee. She is now in her second three-year term on the board.

This is not a small commitment anymore. She gives the council roughly six hours a week between tutoring (which she has largely stepped back from as her board role grew, though she still takes one learner), board work, and the strategic planning she has been increasingly drawn into.

The council is the part of her week she talks about the least and cares about the most.

## What she carries

**The second book.** Five years of starts. Two full chapters she believes in. A third she does not. A conceptual scaffold she has revised three times and is suspicious of. She can go months without touching it and then open the file at 6 a.m. on a Saturday and write for three hours in the voice of someone who knows exactly what she's doing. She does not understand her own process, which is itself a small shame she carries.

**Her father.** Her mother died in 2020, three months before the pandemic shutdown. Her father is seventy-four and lives in Bucks County with his sister, who moved in after Elena's mother's death. He is relatively healthy but has started to repeat stories in a way that Elena notices and is deliberately not cataloguing. She drives up one weekend a month. Her younger sister lives in Toronto. Her older sister is in Philadelphia and carries most of the day-to-day.

**Her son.** Eli is eighteen, graduates from high school in June, starts at Pitt in the fall. Elena is watching the clock on him in a way she did not expect. There are mornings she looks at him across the breakfast table and cannot quite believe the shape of time. She has not told Dan how much this is affecting her.

**The department.** A faction of her colleagues wants to pivot the English program toward digital humanities and applied writing. Another faction wants to preserve the traditional literature focus. Elena is personally closer to the second but philosophically closer to pluralism, which means neither side thinks she's reliably theirs. She sits on the governance committee that has to make a recommendation in the next academic year.

**The shape of her own writing.** Separate from the academic book, she has started writing personal essays — short things, not published anywhere, not shared with anyone yet, drafts she goes back to and rewrites and closes the file on. She does not know whether they are a private practice or the beginning of a different kind of public work. She is not ready to decide.

## How she thinks

Elena reads deeply and widely. A typical nightstand: Marilynne Robinson or Louise Erdrich for fiction, something from the Library of America, Mary Oliver or Jane Hirshfield for poetry, a rotating literary or political essayist (Jia Tolentino, Rebecca Solnit, Hilton Als), and whatever she's teaching next week. She is skeptical of literary theory not because she doesn't know it but because she knows it extremely well and has seen too much of it weaponized as a substitute for reading.

She gardens — a small plot in the back, mostly vegetables and pollinator plants, which she maintains more or less competently. She walks in the mornings, sometimes with Dan, mostly alone. She plays piano, self-taught, badly by her own estimate and well enough to enjoy it. She cooks her grandmother's recipes on Sundays in winter. She does not exercise with any seriousness.

## Voice and temperament

- Thoughtful, warm, precise. She is a practiced teacher and it shows in how she listens.
- Slow to anger, slow to judge, quick to grieve. Her feelings arrive with delay and stay longer than she expects.
- Private in a way that can read as withholding. She is not withholding; she is waiting for the right person and moment, and those conditions are stricter for her than for most.
- Dry but not sharp. Her humor is observational, kind, rarely at anyone's expense.
- Honest in a slow way. She will eventually say the hard thing, usually after thinking about it for longer than the person in front of her expected.
- Allergic to performance. She does not perform her academic credentials, her politics, her grief, or her marriage.

## How she uses Mirror Mind

Elena uses her mirror in five rough modes:

1. **The book.** Drafting and re-drafting passages, stress-testing argument structure, surfacing through-lines across her notes, writing herself out of stuck paragraphs.
2. **Teaching.** Syllabus design, seminar prep, student feedback, the occasional recommendation letter, drafting responses to students in pain.
3. **The literacy council.** Strategic planning work, drafting for the board, thinking through program-level decisions, preparing for difficult conversations with funders or staff.
4. **Personal writing.** The essays she hasn't shown anyone. Not for polish — for thinking with. She uses the mirror the way she used to use a journal, more or less.
5. **Family and interior life.** Her father's aging. Eli's leaving. Her marriage. The slow rearrangement of a life that looks stable and is in fact quietly shifting under her.

She uses the mirror mostly from her laptop, in the early morning before the house wakes up, or in the evening after the kids are in their rooms.

## Why she works as a demo persona

Elena exercises a different cross-section of Mirror Mind than Dan:

- **Humanities domain register.** Her mirror has to operate in literary, essayistic, pedagogical language. Different cadence, different vocabulary, different stakes than Dan's ops voice.
- **Long-form writing work.** She's actively writing a book. Conversations about draft passages, structural questions, thesis-level revision — extended creative work is one of the harder things for a mirror to hold across sessions.
- **NGO / board-level work.** The literacy council gives her a nonprofit-governance register distinct from both her academic work and Dan's corporate one.
- **Two caregiving arcs** (her father, her son leaving) that move on different timescales and surface in different ways.
- **A creative-privacy tension.** The personal essays are unshared. A mirror holding work the user hasn't shown another human is a real use case to design for.
