# The Empty Nest, Incoming

**Status:** active

## Briefing

My son leaves for college in the fall. His sister stays home for two more years. A household of four is about to become a household of three. This is a journey I would have described, six months ago, as a predictable inevitability that would shape our lives modestly without being a subject I needed to think about at length. I was wrong.

The timing: Eli graduates in June, has the summer at home, and leaves for Pitt the third week of August. He will be three hours away by car. He will come home for the October mid-semester break, Thanksgiving, winter break, and probably not much in between. This is standard. This is also not what I prepared myself for.

## Situation

The thing I am noticing is that I am not handling this well, and I have not told anyone that yet.

What I am actually experiencing:

**Attention collapse.** I find myself watching him do ordinary things — eating breakfast, putting his shoes on, taking out the trash — with an attention I did not used to give those moments. I am archiving him. I know this about myself and I cannot fully stop. It is both tender and uncomfortable, and I have not named it out loud to anyone.

**Writing productivity changes.** My morning writing hours, which used to be reliably two hours of work on the book, have started to fragment. I open the file. I read yesterday's paragraph. I find myself thinking about how the kitchen will sound without his music from the other room, and the paragraph goes nowhere. Some mornings I give up at forty-five minutes. I have not yet decided whether to treat this as a phase or a problem.

**Conversation with Dan.** Dan has not said anything about Eli leaving. He is feeling it — I can see the way he lingers at the dinner table some nights — but he is not going to initiate. If I want a conversation with my husband about our son leaving, I will have to open it. I have not opened it. I am not yet sure what I want to say or what I want from him in response. I would like to be past this particular piece of marital shorthand by now, but we are not past it.

**Nora.** My daughter has not said much about her brother leaving. She has her own arc — editor, cross-country, a busy sophomore year — and she is using that busyness as a kind of insulation. I notice it. I have not decided whether to name it to her or let her handle it her own way. My instinct says let her handle it. My other instinct says the conversation I don't have with her now will be harder to have in November, and harder again in February.

**What this is really about.** Some part of me knows this is not only about Eli. It is also about a version of my life that has been organized around the mothering of young-and-then-older children for twenty-one years, and which is about to no longer be organized that way. What comes next is not unclear — I have a book, a career, a marriage, a volunteer commitment, an entire life that was already there. But the shape of the week changes when the thing at the center of the week changes, and I have not yet let myself imagine what the new shape is.

The essays I am writing privately in the morning are, increasingly, about this. I have not yet admitted that to myself, though writing it here seems to make it undeniable.
