# The Department Question

**Status:** active
**Organization:** millersville-university

## Briefing

The department of English is quietly approaching a decision about its own future, and the governance committee I sit on will be asked to make a recommendation to the full department in the next academic year. The shape of the decision: how to reconfigure the major in response to a decade of declining enrollment in traditional literature courses and growing enrollment in the department's newer offerings in digital humanities and applied writing.

Two factions, roughly. The restructure faction wants to redesign the major around concentrations (literary studies, writing studies, digital humanities, public-facing writing), with a smaller shared core. They cite enrollment data, student interest in career-oriented tracks, and the department's need to justify its lines to the dean. The preserve faction wants to hold the current structure, in which all English majors take a common literature core and then elect into emphasis areas. They cite the distinctive intellectual formation the current core provides and the risk that a concentration-based major becomes a writing department with a literature concentration by attrition.

Neither faction is unreasonable. Each has done its homework. The faculty members involved are people I respect.

## Situation

The committee's recommendation is not due for about nine months. The real work of the next year is building enough common ground between the factions that whatever is recommended can actually pass department vote.

My position is genuinely unsettled. My literary formation and the teaching I love most sit squarely in the preserve faction's world. My intellectual commitments — particularly the commitment to pluralism, to not deciding for students in advance what kind of English major they get to be — sit closer to the restructure faction's case. I cannot pick between these by argument alone. The choice will turn on what I think the department can become under each path, which is a speculative question neither side has answered honestly yet.

The specific tensions I'm watching:

**Data and honesty.** The enrollment data, read one way, clearly supports the restructure. Read another way, it tells a more complicated story about what kinds of courses get promoted in first-year advising, how general-education requirements have shifted, and how department resources have been allocated over the last decade. I want the committee to take the more complicated reading seriously before drawing conclusions from the simpler one.

**Collegial strain.** A handful of the strongest literary-studies faculty are starting to read the restructure as an attack. They are not wrong that it would diminish their teaching load. They are also not right that the department is better served by refusing to adapt. I am trying to keep both of those truths visible in the room without flattening either of them.

**The dean.** The new dean has made clear, in several venues, that departments that can't justify their enrollment numbers will face line reductions. This is not a threat directed at us specifically, but it is a context the committee has to think inside. I do not want the department to reorganize itself around what the dean's office wants to hear; I also do not want us to organize ourselves around pretending the pressure is not real.

**My own usefulness.** I have been told by colleagues on both sides that I am one of the few people in the department who could bridge the factions if I chose to. This is a useful position and also a thankless one, and I have not yet decided how much of the next nine months I am willing to give to being that bridge.
