# The Council, the Strategy

**Status:** active
**Organization:** lancaster-literacy-council

## Briefing

A strategic planning process that started out as a routine three-year update and has become the decision-shaped space in which the council's next decade is being determined. I am on the four-person strategic planning subcommittee alongside the executive director, the board chair, and one other long-tenured board member.

The process is loose by design. We meet monthly. We review data, interview stakeholders, propose drafts to the full board. The formal output is a strategic plan document the board will adopt by the end of the calendar year. The informal output is a set of load-bearing decisions that will shape the organization long after the document is filed.

Three interlocking questions are in play. First, how the council responds to the pending reduction in state adult-education funding. Second, whether to apply for a significant three-year national foundation grant whose focus on workforce outcomes fits our work but would require reframing the organization's self-description in ways some of us are wary of. Third, how to prepare for the executive director's eventual retirement, which has not been formally announced but is an open secret among senior board members.

## Situation

We are in the messy middle of the process. Most of the listening work is done; the shape of the strategic direction is not yet set.

The state funding question is the least controversial. Everyone agrees the council cannot replace the lost revenue with philanthropy alone, and that a mix of cost containment and diversification is required. The specifics will hurt — we are likely to have to scale back the workforce track, delay the relocation to a more accessible building that was pencilled into our previous plan, and hold staff levels flat for another two years.

The foundation grant is where the real tension sits. The grant would, if awarded, nearly double the workforce program's budget over three years. It would also commit us to a set of outcome metrics (job placement, wage gains, employer partnerships) that are not how I or the executive director have historically framed the work. The grant's theory of change is adjacent to ours but not identical. My concern is that winning the grant means slowly becoming the organization the grant wants us to be, rather than the one we intended to grow into. The executive director's counter-concern is that refusing the grant means accepting a reduced version of the council for the next decade. Both concerns are real. Neither resolves cleanly.

The executive-director transition is the quiet third rail. We have not yet appointed a search committee because we have not yet been told, formally, that there will be a search. This is producing an awkward shadow process: the strategic plan is being shaped by the person who will not be implementing most of it, without this fact being addressable in the room. I have raised the question once, carefully, in a one-on-one conversation with the board chair. She agreed it needs to be surfaced; neither of us has yet done the surfacing.

My own role in the process has shifted over the last six months. I came in expecting to be a committee member. I am finding myself, uncomfortably, in the position of being the person who asks the harder questions. The executive director has told me, twice, that she is glad I am on the subcommittee for exactly this reason. I take that as a compliment and also as a weight I did not plan to carry.
