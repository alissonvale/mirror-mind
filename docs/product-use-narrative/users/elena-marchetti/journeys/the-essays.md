# The Essays

**Status:** active

## Briefing

For a little over a year I have been writing personal essays. I have not shown them to anyone. I have not submitted them anywhere. I have not told Dan about them, although I suspect he has noticed that my Saturday mornings have taken on a different quality than they used to. I write them in the quiet before the house wakes up, in a plain Markdown file I keep backed up to my Nextcloud and nowhere else.

The subjects, so far: my mother's death and the three months before the pandemic. A particular evening-program tutee who died unexpectedly last spring. The experience of teaching a canonical novel for the eleventh time and finding it new. My father, or rather, my growing inability to distinguish my father from my grief about my father. The piano I play badly and what my family's slow tolerance of it has come to mean. A day on which I noticed Eli was going to leave soon and I had not yet made my peace with it.

Roughly nine drafts of different lengths and degrees of finish. Two I consider close to done. Five I am actively working on. Two I am fairly sure will never be finished.

## Situation

The live question is not "should I publish these." That question is premature; it presupposes that the essays are finished and are of a kind that want publishing, neither of which is established.

The actual live questions:

**What are they for?** I have spent a year writing them without clearly naming their purpose. They are not therapy — I am not writing them to process grief, though that sometimes happens. They are not platform-building — I have no intention of starting a Substack, and the thought of one embarrasses me. They are not academic — they have nothing to do with my book. They seem to be their own thing, which I have not yet named. I think I am writing them because I was a reader before I was a scholar, and writing to a reader has been missing from my working life in a way that has quietly cost me.

**Who, if anyone, do I want to show one to?** Dan would be the obvious first reader. He would be generous with them. He would also not know quite what to say about them, because they are in a register he does not work in and he would feel exposed in the helplessness of not having sharp craft-feedback to offer. I do not want that to be his experience of reading them, and I have not figured out how to avoid it. The other possible first readers — a colleague, a writer friend from graduate school — feel wrong for different reasons. So I have not shown them to anyone, which is increasingly looking less like prudence and more like avoidance.

**The essay about my mother.** The piece I have been most serious about and am most afraid of. It is closer to finished than any of the others. It says things about my mother's last three months that I have not said out loud. I am not sure whether showing it to my sisters would be a gift or a violation. I am not sure whether not showing it to them would be a kindness or a betrayal. I have not resolved this, and I may not resolve it for months.

**The writing is changing me.** I notice I am reading differently. I am reading Joan Didion and Hilton Als more carefully than I did when I was in graduate school. I am noticing sentences in ways I had stopped noticing them. This is either a sign that the essays are good for me, or a sign that I am avoiding the book they are distracting me from, or — most likely — both at once.

The frame I keep trying to hold: the essays do not have to decide what they are for in order to be worth writing. But I have been holding that frame for a year, and at some point "not yet deciding" becomes its own decision. I am aware of that. I am not yet ready to move.
