# The Second Book — *Regional Imagination*

**Status:** active
**Organization:** millersville-university

## Briefing

My second book, working title *Regional Imagination: Place, Labor, and the American Middle Brow*. An argument about mid-twentieth-century American literary regionalism — roughly 1925 to 1960 — and how a set of middlebrow novelists (Dorothy Canfield Fisher, Gladys Hasty Carroll, Ruth Suckow, Josephine Winslow Johnson, others) used regional particularity to do thinking about labor, place, and domestic economy that the critical tradition has largely failed to see because it was dressed in a register the tradition learned to dismiss.

Five years in progress. Two chapters I believe in (the Suckow chapter on Iowa farm women in the Depression, and the Canfield Fisher chapter on New England school reform). One I am not sure about (the Johnson chapter, which keeps wanting to become a different chapter than I have assigned it). Three not yet drafted beyond notes. A conceptual scaffold I have revised three times. A central argument I have also revised at least twice, though I have not always told myself so clearly.

The eventual reader: an intelligent non-specialist who is willing to follow an argument, plus the academic readership the book needs to pass tenure-and-promotion-adjacent review for full professor. Two audiences in tension; the book has to earn both without dissolving into either.

## Situation

I am in a stuck phase of the book's production cycle, which is itself a phase I recognize from the first book.

What is working: the two chapters I believe in have held through multiple revisions. The voice I want the book to have has stabilized over the last eighteen months — slower, more willing to read closely, less theoretical posturing than the dissertation version of me would have allowed.

What is not working: the opening chapter. I have rewritten it four times. Each version tries to introduce a slightly different book. The current draft is closer to the book the other chapters are making than any previous version, but it is still not quite right. I suspect the real issue is that I have been writing the opening as though the book is going to close with an argument I've already made elsewhere, when in fact the book is quietly moving toward a claim I have not yet let myself name.

The Johnson chapter is the other unresolved piece. Josephine Winslow Johnson's *Now in November* is the right novel for the book's argument; my chapter on it, so far, is not the right chapter. I keep writing it as a solo chapter and it wants to be paired with something else. I have not yet figured out what.

The timeline I keep giving myself (finish by the end of next academic year, submit to University of Nebraska by summer) is not a timeline I actually believe. The honest timeline is probably eighteen to twenty-four more months. I have not said this out loud to anyone, including Dan. I am saying it here.

The thing I keep circling and not quite committing to: the book's real argument is not about middlebrow literature recovering what highbrow modernism lost. The argument is quieter than that, and more specific. It is about how a certain kind of attention to place-as-labor shows up in books the tradition has treated as minor, and what it would mean to take that attention as itself an interpretive stance rather than as material for a stance. Which is a much harder book than the one I set out to write, and which may be the book that has been trying to emerge for five years.
