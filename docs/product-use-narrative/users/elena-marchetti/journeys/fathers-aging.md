# My Father Aging

**Status:** active

## Briefing

My father, seventy-four, lives outside Doylestown in Bucks County with my aunt Teresa, who moved in after my mother died in 2020. He is in reasonable health for his age — he walks most days, plays cards twice a week, drives short distances, manages his own medications. He has started repeating stories in a way he did not do two years ago. He has started, less consistently, forgetting names of people he has known for decades. His GP has flagged nothing diagnosable yet. His repeated stories are not, for now, the whole picture; he is still himself, and that self is still finding me and his grandchildren and the part of him that loved my mother.

My sisters: Regina, older, lives in Philadelphia, does most of the day-to-day logistics and the medical appointments, because she is closest; Cristina, younger, lives in Toronto, does the phone calls and the occasional visit, because she can. I sit in the middle geographically and emotionally. I drive up one weekend a month. I call twice a week, sometimes more.

## Situation

This is not an emergency. It is a slow, unmistakable tilt in the floor.

What I notice that I am not fully addressing:

**My father's driving.** He is still legally and practically able to drive. He does not take highway trips anymore. He drives to the grocery store, to my aunt's sister's house three miles away, to the card table at the Knights of Columbus. Regina has told me, twice, that she has noticed him taking longer to react at a stop sign than he used to. We have not had the family conversation.

**Aunt Teresa.** My aunt has been generous beyond description. She moved into the house she did not grow up in so her brother would not be alone. She cooks most of his meals. She notices what we don't see. She is seventy-two herself. The arrangement that is currently working cannot be assumed to work forever. None of us has yet asked her, honestly, whether she wants to be doing this or whether she is doing it because no one else stepped up.

**The group text.** My sisters and I have a text thread that has quietly turned into logistics and photos and almost nothing else. We were closer to each other before our mother died than we are now. I do not know whether this is the natural course of adult-sibling relationships settling, or whether it is a specific failure of the way we are all navigating the slow shape of what is happening with Dad. I think it is some of both.

**My own grief.** My mother's anniversary is in September. Five years this fall. I do not know what I mean to do with it. I have avoided the question. I am raising it with myself now.

**The weekend schedule.** One weekend a month feels both like a lot and like not enough. It is a lot in that it is a significant share of my non-working weekends. It is not enough in that I do not get enough signal between visits to tell how my father is actually doing. The tools my father-in-law-adjacent friends have adopted — cameras, pill dispensers, check-in services — feel premature to me, but they may be less premature than I want to believe.

The frame I keep trying to hold: this is not work I can complete. It is work I am in the middle of, and the middle is the whole of it for as long as it lasts. My job is to be present to what the week actually asks for and to not outrun the arc.
